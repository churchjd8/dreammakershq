I remember the exact moment I realized I didn't know what I didn't know.

We were several years into Suja... past $40 million in revenue, in Whole Foods stores across multiple regions, growing at a pace that made people want to write about us. I was in my office with Nicky, my head of sales, feeling pretty good about where we'd gotten. And I said something that felt like the most obvious thing in the world.

"I can't wait until we achieve full distribution and have nothing to worry about!"

Nicky's face did something I still remember. Like I'd just told her water runs uphill.

"Two years from now," she said, "we'll be fighting tooth and nail to maintain our shelf space. We'll need the right cat man to win."

I stared at her. "What's a cat man?"

The teasing never stopped. Rightfully so.

Here's what I didn't understand at the time: getting into retail is the battle everyone talks about. *Keeping* shelf space is the battle that actually determines who survives.

## What "Cat Man" Actually Means

Category management... "cat man" in the industry... is the discipline of using data, category insights, and retail partnership strategy to win and defend your shelf position. Not just your brand's position. The whole category.

That's the part most founders miss.

Your buyer at Whole Foods or Kroger or Target is not measured on whether your brand does well. They're measured on whether their *category* does well. Organic juice. Refrigerated snacks. Plant-based protein. The category.

If you walk into a buyer meeting talking about your brand and only your brand, you're bringing a knife to a gunfight. A good cat man walks in with data showing how your brand drives category incremental growth. How your SKU's consumer demographic doesn't cannibalize existing brands but expands the category's reach. How the planogram change you're proposing will increase turns per linear foot.

That's a different conversation entirely. And it's the conversation that wins resets.

Jody Cnossen, one of the sharpest retail operators I've ever worked with, used to say it simply: "Jeff, the truth of the business is at the shelf." She was right. Everything we built had to hold up at the shelf level, against real category data, in a real buyer meeting. All the brand story in the world doesn't matter if your category story isn't there.

## The Shelf Space Timeline That Changes How You Think

Most retailers build planograms once a year with a reset cycle roughly every 6 to 12 months. Once you're in, you typically have about nine months to prove you belong.

Nine months. Then they're rebuilding the planogram, and if your velocity numbers don't tell a compelling story, there may not be room for you in the next version.

I learned this the hard way at Rowdy Energy. We paid nearly $1 million in slotting fees to Albertsons. Velocity was significantly below their hurdle level. Eight months later, they discontinued us.

$1 million. Eight months. Gone.

Not because we had a bad product. But because we hadn't done the work to understand how we were performing *relative to the category* and we didn't have the person to build the case for our shelf space before the decision window closed.

The mistake wasn't paying the slotting. The mistake was not treating it like customer acquisition cost and tracking the math relentlessly from day one. (If it costs you $40,000 to get into a 200-store regional chain and your average unit contribution after trade is $1.20, you need to sell 33,000 units just to break even. Most founders never run that number until it's too late.)

## The Velocity Framework Buyers Actually Use

Here's what I give every founder I work with. These are the benchmarks that determine whether you survive a planogram review:

**80% or more of category median**: You reign supreme. You can request off-cycle expansions.

**60-80%**: You're likely growing your SKU count at the next reset.

**50%**: You're doing okay. Not winning, but stable.

**30-50%**: You need a credible plan or you won't make the next reset.

**Below 30%**: Something is seriously wrong. Fix it now, not after the review.

The number that matters isn't your raw units per store per week in isolation. It's your velocity *relative to the category median*. If the average brand in that category does 8 units per store per week and you're doing 6, you're underperforming. Even if 6 feels like a win in absolute terms.

This is what I mean when I say: don't confuse distribution gains with velocity gains. Getting into 500 more doors is exciting. Turning at half the category median inside those 500 doors is just a slow bleed with a bigger footprint.

## The Four Numbers You Need at Your Fingertips

**ACV (All Commodity Volume)**: The percentage of total retail sales in a market that runs through the accounts carrying your product. 1,000 low-traffic stores carry dramatically lower ACV than 300 Walmart supercenters. ACV tells you the *quality* of your distribution, not just the volume.

**Dollars per Point of Distribution (DPOD)**: How efficiently you're generating revenue across your footprint. Improving DPOD is the actual goal of velocity-building activity. This is what separates a strong brand from a brand that just happens to have shelf space.

**USW (Units per Store per Week)**: Your raw velocity. Simple, but foundational. Buyers start here.

**Percent Sold on Promo**: This one is a trap. If 60% of your volume moves only when you're on deal, you have a brand problem, not a velocity problem. You can market your way into trial. You cannot market your way into loyalty. Buyers know this too. If your promoted velocity is 5x your non-promoted velocity... buyers love you on deal. That's not a brand. That's a coupon.

Get comfortable with all four numbers before you walk into any buyer meeting. Not just yours. The category's.

## When You Actually Need to Hire a Cat Man

Most founders don't need a dedicated category manager until they're at $15 to 20 million in revenue. Before that, your sales lead should understand the principles and your broker should be pulling whatever category analysis they have access to.

But here's the thing... by the time you're in multiple major retailers, tracking multiple categories across channels, with planogram reviews coming up on different cycles from different buyers... the cost of *not* having a cat man gets measured in lost shelf space that you worked incredibly hard to get in the first place.

At Suja, I promoted Jason Polinsky to report directly to me after I finally understood what was at stake. He became one of the most valuable people on the team during our entire growth phase. Not because he was the loudest voice in the room. Because he was the most prepared when we walked into buyer meetings.

Dwight Richmond, one of the original visionaries in natural foods at Whole Foods, told me something years later that stuck. Whole Foods' decision to prioritize and expand the cold-pressed juice category wasn't made on gut or on feeling. It was made on syndicated data trends, consumer insights, and forager feedback. The category expanded because someone built the case with data.

That's what cat mans do. They build the case.

## A Word on Data Costs (And What to Do Before You Can Afford It)

This is where it gets frustrating for early-stage founders. Mid-size brands pay $200,000 or more per year for access to SPINS or Nielsen data. Large multinationals pay millions. The tools that power sophisticated category management are often completely out of reach until you hit $20 to 30 million in revenue.

So what do you do before that?

You use what you have. Retailer scan data. Distribution reports. Your own shipment data. DTC analytics if you have a direct channel. You ask your broker to pull whatever they have access to inside their retailer relationships. You show up to buyer meetings with the data you can access and tell a sharp story with it.

The mistake isn't having imperfect data. The mistake is walking into a planogram review unprepared because the perfect data wasn't available.

## The Bottom Line

Around 2018, a Whole Foods buyer told us she had 62 cold-pressed juice brands requesting meetings. She couldn't possibly see them all.

The brands that got meetings weren't necessarily the biggest. They were the ones with the strongest category story. The ones who could show, credibly, what their presence did for the category... not just for themselves.

Getting on shelf is a pitch. Staying on shelf is a performance. And performance gets measured in data.

Get your velocity numbers clean. Know where you stand relative to the category median. Build the case before your buyer has to. Understand that the buyer is not your partner in keeping you on shelf... they're a partner in growing the category, and your job is to make that case compelling.

And if you're still not sure what a cat man does... go find Nicky. She'll explain it to you.
