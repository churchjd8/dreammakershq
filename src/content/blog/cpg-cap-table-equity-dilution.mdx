It was July 2015. We were in the final stages of closing what would become one of the most significant deals in the natural beverage space. Coca-Cola valued Suja at $300 million. They were writing a $90 million check. Goldman Sachs was writing another $60 million. We were sitting on what looked like the deal of a lifetime.

And then our attorney walked me through the preference stack.

The way ACG's terms were structured... nearly all of Coca-Cola's $90 million investment would flow straight to them. Not to the angel investors who had believed in us before we had a dollar of institutional money. Not to the founders who had built the thing from scratch. To a private equity fund that had been in the deal for less than 12 months.

I remember sitting with that information and feeling something shift. This wasn't what any of us signed up for. These angels had been with us for three years. Some of them had written checks before we had a co-manufacturer, before we had consistent shelf life, before any of this felt real. And now they were going to get almost nothing from a $90 million investment by the most recognizable beverage company on the planet.

That's when I decided we couldn't close the deal as structured.

## The Cap Table Nobody Talks About

You don't hear a lot of honest talk about cap tables in CPG circles. Founders talk about their raise. They talk about their valuation. They post about their latest round on LinkedIn. But the actual mechanics of the cap table... that's a private conversation. Usually because people don't fully understand what they signed, and the realization comes at the worst possible time.

I've seen founders sell their company for $200 million and walk away with 4% of that. That's $8 million. Before taxes. After 10 years of work. 80-hour weeks, constant stress, real sacrifice... and $8 million is what they have to show for it.

That's not failure. That's poor equity management. And it's more common than you'd think.

Here's what nobody tells you in the early days: equity feels cheap when you're desperate for validation. You're pre-revenue, maybe pre-product. An advisor wants 2%. A consultant wants 1%. A friend who introduces you to a buyer wants 0.5%. Each piece feels small. And then one day you're trying to close a Series B and you own 18% of your own company, and you realize those small pieces added up to a lot.

## What Healthy Dilution Actually Looks Like

Here's a framework I wish someone had handed me before my first fundraise.

**Per round: don't dilute more than 10-15%.** Every round should increase the company's valuation meaningfully enough that the dollars you're raising don't crush your percentage ownership. If you're raising $1M and giving away 30% of your company, the math is telling you something. Either your valuation is too low, or you're raising too little money at the wrong time.

**Through the life of the company: try to maintain 50% or more through your first two rounds.** This gives you enough runway to dilute in later rounds without ending up in single digits at exit. Once you're in single digits, hope is not a strategy.

**Red zone: if a management team member gets 5-10% of equity too early, the math gets dangerous quickly.** Equity without vesting is a time bomb. I've seen companies hand out 5% to an early hire who leaves in 18 months. That equity is gone. And every future investor is going to ask about it.

Healthy equity management isn't stingy. It's strategic. You want the people who matter most to own enough that they're fully aligned. But "the people who matter most" is a much smaller list than founders tend to think in year one.

## The Preference Stack Problem

Let me go back to the Coca-Cola deal for a second. Because this is where a lot of founders get hurt and don't even see it coming.

When institutional investors come in, they almost always come in with **preference provisions**. Liquidation preferences, participating preferred terms, anti-dilution provisions. These are standard. They're not inherently bad. They're how investors protect downside risk on early checks.

But here's what founders often miss: those provisions compound. Each round stacks on the last. And by the time you have three or four institutional rounds in, the preference stack can mean that the first $50 or $80 million of a successful exit goes entirely to investors before founders and angels see a dollar.

ACG had been in the deal for less than a year. Their preference provisions were structured aggressively. And in a deal where Coca-Cola was investing $90 million, the math was going to work out very badly for the people who had taken the most risk earliest.

I pushed back. We went out and found Goldman Sachs. Their $60 million investment changed the structure enough that our angel investors could sell roughly 40% of their holdings at about a 3.4x return. They kept the other 60% of their equity intact. Later, when Suja eventually sold to Paine Schwartz for approximately $300 million, those angels got a second bite. Their total return ended up around 10x.

That's not a coincidence. That's the result of someone paying attention to the cap table when it mattered.

## The Equity Mistakes That Cost Founders Millions

I've been in and around CPG fundraising for more than 30 years. I've raised money across 40+ rounds, 8 companies. Here's what I see go wrong most often.

**Casual equity promises.** "Hey, if you help us get into Whole Foods, I'll give you half a percent." Fine. But do that five times and you've given away 2.5% before you've done a single formal raise. Keep going and you'll wonder where your ownership went.

**Equity without vesting schedules.** If you're giving equity to anyone who isn't a co-founder, it needs a vesting schedule. Standard is 4 years with a 1-year cliff. No cliff, and someone walks at month 3 with fully vested equity they didn't earn.

**Over-diluting in early rounds.** When you're raising your seed, the valuation feels like a negotiation about abstract numbers. It isn't. It's a negotiation about what percentage of your company you're selling. A $500K raise at a $1M post-money valuation is 50% of your company. The same raise at a $5M post-money is 10%. Know what you're giving away before you agree to terms.

**Ignoring the preference stack.** I know this feels like advanced level territory when you're trying to close your first check. But even at the seed stage, the terms matter. Participating preferred vs. non-participating preferred. 1x liquidation preference vs. 2x. These aren't boilerplate. They're decisions with real consequences at exit.

**No equity strategy at all.** This is the most common one. Founders raise when they need money, give away what they have to, and hope for the best. Hope is not a strategy. If you don't have a deliberate plan for how your cap table should look at Series A, Series B, and exit... you're not managing it, you're reacting to it.

## The Internal Champion You Also Need

One thing the Coca-Cola deal taught me that goes beyond mechanics: deals often hinge on people, not terms.

We had Mike Saint John, then President of Minute Maid Juice, as our internal champion at Coca-Cola. Without someone inside a potential acquiring company willing to take reputational risk on your behalf, deals quietly die. No one kills them. They just stop moving.

The same principle applies on your cap table. You want investors who are genuinely aligned with your success. Not just on the upside, but in the hard moments. The investor who pushed for aggressive preferences in month 11 wasn't going to be your advocate when the board got hard. The angel who'd been with you for 3 years would walk through fire. Know the difference.

Your cap table is not just a financial document. It's a map of who your actual allies are.

## What to Do Right Now

If you're early stage:

1. **Get a CPG-experienced attorney before your first raise.** Not a general business attorney. Someone who has seen dozens of CPG term sheets. The difference between participating and non-participating preferred can mean millions at exit.

2. **Build your equity budget before you start giving any away.** How much will you need for the founding team? Key early hires? Advisors? Investors across four or five rounds? Work backward from a target ownership at exit and build toward it.

3. **Put everything in writing.** Every single promise. Even to family. Especially to family. "I've seen siblings stop speaking over equity misunderstandings." Don't assume a handshake agreement is anything more than a relationship liability.

4. **Track renewal dates, vesting schedules, and preference terms in a centralized document.** Your cap table should live somewhere you look at regularly, not just when a new investor asks for it.

5. **Don't give equity to solve problems that equity can't solve.** Cash flow problems need cash solutions. Relationship problems need relationship solutions. Equity is for building alignment with people who will be with you for the long haul.

---

## The Truth About Ownership

Here's the thing about your cap table that most people only understand in hindsight: it's a scoreboard. Every decision you make about equity is a decision about who gets credit for what you're building. Who shares in the upside. Who has power over the direction of the company.

A clean, well-managed cap table attracts better investors. It gives you more flexibility in tough moments. It keeps your incentives aligned with the people who matter. And when an exit happens... it's the difference between a life-changing outcome and a disappointing footnote.

Gross margin determines destiny on the income statement. The cap table determines destiny at exit.

Manage both with equal attention.
