There's a guy in my world I call "the green juice guy."

His name is Eric Ethans, and when he was in his early twenties, he was making cold-pressed juice at a raw food restaurant in Encinitas, California. He'd created about 10 different recipes. And every day he'd load up those bottles and deliver them door-to-door in his neighborhood... on his skateboard.

That was his consumer research. Informal, sure. But it was real. He was watching people drink his product. Seeing their faces. Hearing their reactions. Getting repeat orders. Or not.

When my partner James Brennan brought one of those bottles to my office, I refused it. Multiple times. I was busy. I didn't want kale. I had other things going on. But eventually I tried it.

Three ingredients: apple, kale, lemon.

I called my wife Linda that night. "Honey, if I, with my unrefined Midwestern palate, can love a product with kale in it... then anyone on the planet can." That became Suja's Green Supreme. That gut reaction? It was consumer research in its most raw, honest form.

Here's the thing most founders get wrong. They do the research backwards.

They spend $250,000 on packaging, co-packing runs, slotting fees, and broker agreements. Then they find out... at retail... that the price point is too high, the flavor profile is off, the format doesn't work for how people actually shop. By then you've already written the checks. Already made the commitments.

The research should happen before the investment. Not after.

---

## "Customers Don't Reward Passion. They Reward Value."

I've said this a thousand times and I'll keep saying it. Passion is the fuel. Discipline is the engine. When it comes to your product, your consumers are ruthlessly honest and your friends are not.

So how do you actually know if anyone wants what you're selling? Here's what I've learned across 8 companies and 30+ years of building brands.

---

## 1. Stand at the Shelf. Literally.

This is the advice that sounds too simple to be useful. It's not.

Before you launch, before you finalize your formula, before you commit to packaging... go stand next to your competitor's product at retail. Watch what happens. Watch who picks it up. Watch who puts it back down. Talk to them.

Jody Cnossen, one of the smartest retail minds I've worked with, used to tell me: "Jeff, the truth of the business is at the shelf." She was right. No focus group can replicate what you observe when a real shopper is standing in the aisle, making a real purchase decision, with real money.

Ask them why they're picking up that product. Ask what they wish it did differently. Ask if they've tried something similar. You'll get more useful intel in two hours at Whole Foods than you'll get in a $20,000 market research report.

No budget required. Just show up.

---

## 2. Read the Reviews. Your Competitor's Reviews, Not Yours.

Go to Amazon. Find your top 3 competitors. Read every 1-star and 2-star review in the category.

You're not looking for complaints. You're looking for unmet needs. You're looking for the thing people keep saying they wish existed.

"I love this product but the packaging leaks." "Great flavor but too much sugar." "Perfect for me but my husband won't touch it because it looks like medicine."

Every one of those is a product brief. Every one of those is a positioning opportunity. And it cost you nothing except the hour it took to read them.

I had a founder show me a product once and tell me their brand was "completely different" from the competition. I spent 20 minutes reading Amazon reviews in their category and came back with four specific things consumers were begging someone to fix. His product didn't fix any of them. That's a problem.

---

## 3. Test with $100 Before Spending $250,000.

Most founders I talk to have never heard of Pickfu.com. Which surprises me because it's one of the most useful tools in the early-stage CPG toolkit.

You can put your product concept, your packaging design, your name, your price point... in front of 100 matched consumers (matched to your target demographic) for roughly $100 and 48 hours.

One hundred real people. Your actual target audience. Answering questions about whether they'd buy your product, what confuses them, what excites them.

That's $100 versus $250,000.

I'm not saying Pickfu replaces the need to actually launch and test in market. Nothing replaces that. But before you finalize a name, before you lock a package design, before you commit to a formula... $100 is a very cheap reality check.

I've watched founders print 50,000 units of packaging before realizing their name didn't resonate or their price point was 40% above what consumers expected. They found out the hard way. You don't have to.

---

## 4. The Stranger Test.

Here's a simple one I use when evaluating positioning. Tell a stranger (someone who doesn't know you, doesn't know your brand, owes you nothing) what your product is in one sentence. Then ask two questions:

"Does this sound like something you'd try?"

"What do you think it costs?"

If their assumptions are aligned with what you're actually selling... you have clarity. If they're wildly off... you have a positioning problem. Better to know now than at retail, when the buyer is asking the same questions and you don't have a good answer.

This test is free. It takes five minutes. Most founders skip it because they're afraid of the answer.

---

## 5. Don't Make the Billboard Mistake.

Early on at Suja, we ran billboards in Manhattan while we only had 4 Whole Foods stores in the entire city.

We created awareness with nowhere to convert it.

I tell founders this story all the time because it illustrates something important. You can't validate consumer demand by creating awareness in a vacuum. You have to be able to convert the interest into a purchase, measure the repeat, and actually learn something. Otherwise you've spent money on noise, not insight.

The most dangerous "consumer insight" is the one that shows people "love" your product at a demo... but never buy it again. Which brings me to the only number that actually matters.

---

## 6. Repeat Rate Is the Only Metric That Tells the Truth.

I don't care how many people tried your product at the farmers market. I don't care how many units you moved at a tasting event. I care about one number: what percentage of first-time buyers come back?

The target is 40-50%. If you're under 20%... you don't have a brand. You have a curiosity.

"You can market your way into trial. You cannot market your way into loyalty."

This is the most important thing I've ever said about CPG, and it took me years to truly understand it. Trial is a marketing problem. Repeat is a product problem. No amount of influencer spend or demo budget fixes a product that people try once and never buy again.

Before you spend $250K on a launch, can you get your product into 20 real people's hands, ask them to try it for 30 days, and see who comes back? That's your earliest proof of concept. That's consumer research in its most honest form.

If less than 20% of those people rebuy within the first month, stop. Reformulate. Reprice. Reposition. Do something different before you scale a broken product into a $500K mistake.

---

## The Research Jeff Is Actually Talking About

None of this requires a research firm. None of it requires a big budget. What it requires is intellectual honesty.

Jessica Pratt, the broker who helped get Suja into Whole Foods, didn't know it at the time... but her visceral reaction when I mentioned the cold-pressed juice brand over dinner at Jimbo's was consumer research. She was already making green juice at home every day. When she lit up the way she did, I knew we had something real.

Eric on his skateboard was validating velocity before he even knew what velocity meant. My own gut reaction when I finally stopped refusing the bottle was consumer insight.

Start with truth, not excitement. The founders who skip this step usually spend the most money finding out the hard way what a $100 Pickfu survey would have told them for free.

"Passion can be one of the most dangerous things a founder brings to a business if it isn't paired with discipline."

The research is the discipline. Do it before you write the check.
