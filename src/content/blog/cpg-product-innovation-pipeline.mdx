The call came in the spring of 2019. Trader Joe's had spotted the celery juice trend building across social media and wanted product on shelves nationwide. Fast.

Their preferred path was private label. But their own process for that would have taken 6 months minimum. They called Suja instead.

From that first call to product hitting Trader Joe's shelves nationally: 41 days.

That's what an innovation machine looks like. Not a perfect process. Not a 16-slide stage-gate review. A system that moves.

---

Here's what I see when I work with CPG founders on their innovation pipeline. Two camps. Almost always.

Camp one: the perfectionists. They spend 18 months refining a product before it ever touches a consumer's hand. They test and re-test. They agonize over every ingredient. They wait for the perfect moment. And then... the market moves on without them.

I met a couple at Expo East years ago. They'd started a juice company around the same time we launched Suja. Smart people. Thoughtful. Incredibly careful. By the time we were doing $44 million in revenue in 2014, they were stuck at a fraction of that number. Their desire for perfection kept them from seizing every window that opened.

Camp two: the scattered innovators. They launch everything. New SKUs every quarter. Line extensions that have nothing to do with their core. A salad dressing to go with the protein bar. A kids' line because "it's a big market." They confuse activity with progress. And then their gross margin collapses and they wonder why.

Both camps die slow deaths.

The answer isn't in the middle. It's somewhere different entirely.

---

## Innovate Fast. Cut Hard. Repeat.

At Suja, we launched 275 SKUs in our first seven years. That's roughly one new SKU every nine days. Sounds reckless, right?

We aggressively discontinued all but 55.

Of the original top-10 SKUs we launched, only one was still in the top 10 a decade later. One.

The lesson: you cannot predict which innovations will win. What you can control is how quickly you learn and how ruthlessly you cut when the market gives you the answer.

When Coca-Cola invested in Suja in July 2015, they brought in their "Stages and Gates" innovation framework. Beautiful process. Very thorough. Typically takes 16 to 18 months from concept to shelf. Our process took 6 weeks. Coca-Cola loved our speed. Their own internal system couldn't touch it. That gap... that gap is where small brands can out-maneuver large ones if they're paying attention.

Speed isn't the goal. Learning is. Speed is just how you get there faster.

## The 80/20 Rule Applied to Product Development

There's a principle I come back to constantly: 80% of your results come from 20% of your efforts. That's true in operations. It's true in sales. It is absolutely true in product innovation.

Getting a product 80% ready into market for real consumer feedback almost always beats perfecting it in isolation. A good product that evolves based on what customers tell you will outperform a "perfect" product that is rigid and unresponsive. Every single time.

This is hard for founders to accept because it requires tolerating imperfection in public. But the consumer doesn't care about your R&D journey. They care about whether the product delivers on the promise.

Our Costco relationship is a good example. We ran test-and-learn rotations in 10-week windows. Each failed rotation got an X. Each successful one got a star. We kept the cadence regardless. Instead of taking 3 extra months to perfect each offer, we let the market decide. And we were the first to tell the buyer when something needed to be replaced... not them telling us. Us telling them.

That's how you build credibility with a buyer. Not by being perfect. By being honest and fast.

## When to Enter a Category (Timing Is Strategy)

The innovation question isn't just "what should we build?" It's "when should we enter?"

At Suja, we entered the cold-pressed juice market as roughly the third or fourth credible national player. Evolution Fresh and Blueprint Cleanse had already pioneered the category. Within 90 days of our Whole Foods launch in Southern California, Evolution was acquired by Starbucks and Blueprint was acquired by Hain Celestial. While they were integrating and distracted, we stayed focused and scaled.

We didn't invent the category. We refined it. At exactly the right moment.

Here's the principle: the 3rd, 4th, or 5th entrant in a category is the sweet spot. You benefit from pioneers who already proved the concept. You avoid the "tax" of educating consumers from scratch. But if you're the 10th or 15th entrant... a Whole Foods buyer told me years later that she had 62 cold-pressed juice brands requesting meetings. She couldn't possibly see them all.

"It's rarely ideal to be first to market, and it's dangerous to be last."

Timing is a strategy. The best founders think about category maturity the way an investor thinks about market cycles.

## The Most Expensive Mistake in CPG Innovation

Here's where most founders get hurt. They innovate away from their core identity.

We did this at Suja with kombucha. Technically, we were good. We'd actually solved the alcohol consistency challenge that plagues most kombucha producers. But consumers didn't see Suja as a kombucha brand. We were a cold-pressed juice company. And kombucha, which we outsourced, was running at roughly 12% gross margin.

Meanwhile, our wellness shots... produced internally, built on the same cold-pressed technology that made Suja famous... were generating gross margins close to 60%.

When we made the decision to exit kombucha and double down on wellness shots, company-wide gross margin moved from roughly 32% to roughly 40%. On a $100 million revenue base, that's not a rounding error.

"Gross margin is everything."

The innovation test isn't just "will consumers buy this?" It's "does this build on what we already do well, and does the margin structure support the business we're trying to build?" Expansion that drifts from your core identity weakens the brand. Expansion that deepens your core identity strengthens it.

You can grow revenue with the wrong product mix. Revenue without margin is ego.

---

## Always Have a Plan B

One more thing that most founders skip: when you're launching a new product line, always develop an alternative in parallel.

At least half the time, Plan A doesn't materialize the way you expected. Plan B gives you somewhere to go. It lets you preserve shelf space, maintain retailer confidence, and buy time while you refine your approach.

This isn't pessimism. This is how serious operators think. Dream boldly. Plan soberly.

A retailer giving you a shot on a new innovation is a gift. Showing up empty-handed when Plan A stumbles is how you burn that gift to the ground. A viable Plan B is how you protect the relationship... and your spot on shelf.

## The Practical Innovation Playbook

Here's what it actually looks like in practice:

**Set your velocity hurdle first.** Before you launch anything, know what velocity you need to stay on shelf. If a SKU isn't hitting 50% of category median within 12 weeks, it needs a plan or it needs to go. Retailers run planogram resets roughly every 12 months. You have about 9 months to prove you belong before the next review cycle.

**Use Costco's 13-week rotation as an innovation testing lab.** The rotation model is one of the most underused low-commitment testing platforms in CPG. High consumer signal, contained risk, strong feedback loop.

**Invite retailers into the process early.** The more a buyer feels ownership over a concept, the more internal advocacy you'll get. Share early formulations. Ask for honest feedback. Let them feel like co-creators instead of just distribution channels.

**Use short-term exclusives to secure distribution.** A 3 to 6 month channel exclusive in exchange for guaranteed placement is often a fair trade early in an innovation launch. You get the distribution. They get the story. Everybody wins.

**Cut before you're forced to.** The brands that win do their own cutting. They don't wait for the buyer to pull the product. They proactively raise underperforming SKUs with buyers and come in with solutions. That discipline is what earns you the credibility to launch the next thing.

CPG is a "penny profit" business. Every SKU you carry has overhead attached to it. Complexity costs money. Simplicity is a margin strategy.

Move fast. Cut what isn't working. Build on your core. And never let the fear of imperfection keep you from learning what the market is already trying to tell you.
