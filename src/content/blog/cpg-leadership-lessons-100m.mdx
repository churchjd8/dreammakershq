Building Suja Juice from zero to $100M in revenue in five years taught me more about leadership than my Harvard MBA ever did. And I don't say that lightly... I'm grateful for every minute of that education. But the classroom and the arena are very different places.

These are the seven leadership lessons that made the difference. Every one of them was paid for in equity, dilution, sleepless nights, or some combination of the three.

## 1. Your Brand Needs a Soul

If your company doesn't have a "soul," you're just selling products. And products are commodities.

A soul is the reason your team shows up every morning with fire. It's the reason consumers choose you over the 47 other options on the shelf. It's the thing that can't be copied by a bigger competitor with more resources and a larger marketing budget.

At Suja, our soul was making plant-powered nutrition accessible to everyone. That wasn't a marketing tagline. It was a belief system that drove every decision... from sourcing to pricing to who we hired. When your brand has a genuine soul, it attracts the right people and repels the wrong ones. That's a competitive advantage no amount of money can buy.

## 2. Don't Naively Believe Your Board Will Support Your Vision All the Way

This one stings. But it's reality, and you need to hear it before you learn it the hard way.

Your board's job is to maximize shareholder value. Your job is to build a great company. Those two things align... until they don't. I've seen boards push for a premature sale because the return was "good enough." I've seen them replace founders with "professional management" right when the founder's vision was about to pay off.

Your board is not your friend. They're your fiduciary partners. Understand the difference.

Build board relationships early and often. Communicate proactively. Understand what each board member's ideal outcome looks like (and it's different for each of them). And always maintain enough influence... through voting rights, performance, or both... to protect your vision when it matters most.

## 3. Investors Want Profitability Sooner Than You Think

The days of "grow at all costs" in CPG are over. Today's investor community wants to see a path to profitability much sooner than they did even a few years ago. If your pitch is "we'll be profitable in year seven," you're going to have a very hard time raising money.

And you know what? This shift is actually healthy for founders. It forces discipline. It forces you to build a real business instead of a growth-at-all-costs story that requires endless capital to survive.

Build your financial model with EBITDA breakeven by year 3-4. Show investors that you can be profitable if you choose to be, even if you're reinvesting for growth. That optionality is what gives them confidence.

## 4. Create Synergistic Relationships with Everyone in the Value Chain

CPG is a relationship business. Your suppliers, retailers, distributors, brokers... they're not vendors. They're partners. And the brands that create genuine win-win relationships with their value chain partners are the ones that get preferential treatment when it matters.

At Suja, we treated our retail partners as true collaborators. We shared data. We invested in their success. We made their category managers look good (this matters more than you think). In return, we got better shelf placement, more promotional support, and first-mover advantage on new opportunities.

Every interaction with a partner should leave them better off. If you're always extracting value and never creating it, you'll find yourself with fewer and fewer allies. And in this business, allies are everything.

## 5. Prior CEO Experience Matters... But It's Not Everything

Ideally, your CEO has scaled a brand before. They've made the mistakes, learned the lessons, and can navigate the chaos. But not every founding team has that luxury, and that's okay.

If you don't have prior CEO experience, certain behaviors become non-negotiable: intellectual humility, the ability to make decisions with incomplete information, a bias toward speed over perfection, and the self-awareness to hire people smarter than you in areas where you're weak. You don't have to know everything. You have to know what you don't know.

## 6. The Fundamentals of Management Actually Matter

This sounds obvious. But you'd be shocked how many high-growth CPG brands ignore basic management practices. Regular one-on-ones. Clear performance expectations. Documented processes. Financial reviews. These aren't corporate bureaucracy... they're the foundation that keeps your company from falling apart as you scale.

At Suja, the periods where we were most chaotic were the periods where we'd let fundamentals slide because we were "too busy growing." That's exactly backwards. The faster you grow, the more the fundamentals matter. That pace changes you... and without fundamentals to anchor you, it can change you in the wrong direction.

## 7. Find the Kingmakers and Connectors

Kingmakers do exist. In every CPG segment, there are a handful of people who can open doors that would otherwise take you years to unlock. Industry veterans, influential buyers, well-connected investors, trade association leaders. Connect with the "connectors" in your segment and doors will open.

Finding and building relationships with these people was one of the highest-ROI activities I've ever engaged in. One introduction to the right buyer at the right time can change the trajectory of your entire company. One endorsement from a respected industry figure gives you credibility that no marketing campaign can match.

Go to every industry event. Join trade associations. Ask your best mentors who they know. And when you connect with a kingmaker, lead with value... don't just ask for favors. Build a genuine relationship. The doors will open.
