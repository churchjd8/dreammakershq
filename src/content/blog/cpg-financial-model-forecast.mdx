Here's October 2018. Suja has crossed $100 million in revenue. We're in 30,000-plus retail locations. Forbes called us the second most promising business in America. Inc. 5000 put us 13th fastest-growing company in the country.

And Todd Fisher, our CFO, is calling me every week to tell me we have less than $100,000 in the bank.

Not $100,000 available to deploy. $100,000 total. On a $100 million company.

That number still makes my stomach drop when I say it out loud.

How does that happen? How does a brand that size end up managing cash by the hour?

The honest answer: we had a financial model. We just weren't living in it.

## The fundraising model vs. the operating model

Most CPG founders build a financial model exactly once. Right before a pitch. Revenue hockey-sticks to the right. Gross margin magically climbs to 50% by year three. EBITDA appears on schedule, like a train that always runs on time.

Then the deck closes. The raise happens (if it does). And the model goes into a Google Drive folder where it slowly becomes fiction.

That's not a financial model. That's a fundraising prop.

The founders who survive the hard years... the cash crunches, the manufacturing overruns, the retail delistings... are the ones who build a model they actually live inside. Every single week.

There's a phrase I've used since our roughest season: "Hope is not a forecasting model." I mean that literally. When you're operating on hope instead of numbers, you don't know you're in trouble until the water's already at your knees.

## Start with the structure

A CPG financial model isn't complicated. It has five components, and you need all five:

1. **Revenue model** — how many doors, what velocity per store, what price
2. **Gross margin model** — COGS, trade spend, freight as a percentage of net revenue
3. **Operating expense model** — headcount, marketing, G&A
4. **Cash flow model** — accounts receivable, payable, inventory turns, capex
5. **Scenario model** — base case, bear case, bull case

The order matters. Most founders start with the P&L. Wrong move. Start with the cash flow. Because CPG is a "Penny Profit" business where the pennies matter, and more importantly, where the cash moves before the margin does. You can show a profitable month on paper while your bank account is hemorrhaging.

## Build it bottom-up

This is the most important structural choice you'll make: bottom-up or top-down.

Top-down modeling starts with a market. "The organic beverage market is $10 billion. We'll capture 1% of that in year three." That's not a model. That's a number selected to sound plausible.

Bottom-up starts with the shelf. How many stores will carry you in month one? What's a realistic velocity — units per store per week — for a brand at your stage, in your category, at your price point? Multiply those two numbers together. That's your revenue.

From there, layer in gross margin. At Suja, we started at 28%. We were targeting 40% by year two and 50% by year three or four. Every incremental point of gross margin at scale is real money. When you're at $100 million in revenue, a single point of gross margin improvement is worth a million dollars.

"Gross margin determines destiny." I've said it a thousand times. Not as a slogan. As an operating reality.

Most institutional investors won't even look at you if your gross margins aren't above 40% with a credible path to 50%. That's not a soft preference. That's a hard filter. And your model is where you prove it, or where you realize you can't.

## The four numbers every CPG founder needs to know cold

Any time. Any place. No hesitation.

**What is my gross margin this month?** Not last quarter. This month. If you have to look it up, you're flying blind.

**What is my trade spend as a percentage of gross revenue?** This is where margin goes to disappear. If you're running above 25-30% in conventional grocery and you're not actively working to bring it down, you've got a structural problem.

**What is my velocity per store per week in my top accounts?** Velocity determines whether you stay on shelf. Not distribution. Velocity. Don't confuse distribution gains with velocity gains. That confusion has killed more promising CPG brands than bad products ever did.

**How many weeks of runway do I have?** I raise for at least 18 months. That's been my rule across 44 fundraising rounds over 30 years. If your answer to this question is "less than 12 months," you should be raising right now, not reading this.

## The cash gap that kills brands

Here's what almost no one talks about openly: in CPG, growth consumes cash before it produces it.

When you add a major retailer, you have to produce product, build inventory, ship it, wait 30-90 days for payment, and absorb deductions — all before a single dollar of that revenue hits your bank account. The faster you grow, the worse this gets.

We had a $15 million manufacturing overrun building our Oceanside plant. That single cost overage prevented us from deploying $10 million in marketing funds we had earmarked from the Coca-Cola investment. In the same window, Campbell's and Pepsi both entered the cold-pressed juice category. We had capital allocated to fight back on consumer awareness. We couldn't deploy it.

A properly built scenario model would have flagged that risk. If we'd been stress-testing our capital needs against construction cost overruns — running base, bear, and bull assumptions — we would have seen the downstream consequences before they landed.

The Rule of Twos applies to financial modeling just like it applies to everything else in this business: everything takes twice as long and costs twice as much. Build that assumption in before you start, not after the overrun hits.

## How to use the model operationally

Your model shouldn't live in a Google Drive folder. It should live in your weekly operating rhythm.

Here's the cadence that works:

**Weekly:** Review actual cash position against projection. Review deductions and chargebacks that came through. Compare actual velocity reports from top accounts against model assumptions. If something moved, know why.

**Monthly:** Compare actuals to budget across all five components. Identify variances. Update your rolling 13-week cash forecast. Don't skip the 13-week. It's the single most useful piece of financial hygiene a CPG founder can build.

**Quarterly:** Run your scenarios. What does the model say if your top retailer resets you to fewer doors? What if raw material costs go up 15%? What if your raise takes six months longer than projected? (The Rule of Twos says it probably will.)

The goal isn't precision. The goal is eliminating surprise. Surprises in CPG are expensive. The founders who thrive are the ones who see bad news early enough to do something about it.

## What investors actually want to see

When you're in a raise, the model becomes a communication tool. And it needs to show a few specific things.

Bottom-up revenue tied to specific retailer assumptions, not percentages of a TAM. Gross margin trajectory with the COGS assumptions that support it. Trade spend as its own line item with channel-specific benchmarks. A clear 18-month cash need with use of proceeds broken out by category.

For emerging CPG brands doing $3-20 million in revenue, you're typically looking at a 3-4x trailing twelve-month multiple on net revenue. That's the valuation framework. Know it. Know where you land on it. Know what levers move it.

And here's the other thing: when you're in a room with an investor, they can tell within five minutes whether you live in your model or just built it. The founders who live in it answer questions in specifics, immediately, without fishing for the slide. The ones who just built it say, "Let me check with my CFO."

That's the tell. Know your numbers.

## The year we ran it weekly

After Coca-Cola decided not to move forward with the full acquisition in July 2018, Todd and I spent the next year running Suja on weekly cash projections. We were burning more than $10 million a year. $40 million in secured debt was coming due that October. At times we were operating with less than $100,000 in the bank on a $100 million revenue company.

We didn't fix that by building a better pitch deck.

We cut marketing by more than $4 million. Revenue grew roughly 10% that year anyway. Strong brands with loyal customers don't always need massive spending to grow. The model showed us which cuts were safe and which would do real damage.

We eliminated low-margin outsourced products. We shifted toward wellness shots, which we produced internally at around 60% gross margin versus the 12% we were getting on outsourced kombucha. That single product mix shift moved company-wide gross margins from 32% to 40%.

The model didn't save the company. The decisions did. But the decisions only got made because the model showed us exactly where we were.

"Revenue without margin is ego." We had revenue. We needed margin. The model told us where to find it.

Dream boldly. Plan soberly. Build a model you actually live in.
