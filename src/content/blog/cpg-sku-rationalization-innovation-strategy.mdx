I remember the exact moment I got called out by one of my own board members.

We were at a pre-board dinner. About 14 people at the table - not exactly a private setting. We were four years into building Suja. We'd crossed $44 million in revenue. We were the fastest-growing cold-pressed juice brand in the country. And we were launching new SKUs like our lives depended on it.

That's when one of our board members leaned back in his chair and said: "Jeff, don't you see that what you think is disruptive innovation is just causing unnecessary churn?"

I laughed it off. But the question stuck.

Here's what I didn't say at the table: we had launched 275 SKUs in seven years. That's roughly one new product every nine days. We had aggressively discontinued all but 55 of them. Only one of our original top-ten SKUs was still in the top ten a decade later.

So... was he right?

---

## The Two Failure Modes Nobody Talks About

There are two ways to blow up your CPG business through product decisions, and most founders swing hard toward one of them.

**Failure Mode #1: You launch too many SKUs trying to find the winner.**

Your retail partners get confused. Your operations team is stretched thin. Your working capital is buried in slow-moving inventory. And instead of building real velocity behind your hero products, you're dividing attention across 15 items that none of your consumers really love yet.

**Failure Mode #2: You fall in love with your launch lineup and never cut anything.**

You protect underperformers out of emotional attachment. You keep funding products that aren't moving because you already paid to develop them. You never build the kind of concentrated velocity that retailers actually reward.

The founders who build lasting brands understand something most miss: innovation is a process, not a commitment. You launch to learn. You keep what works. You kill what doesn't - and you do it fast.

---

## What Innovation Actually Looks Like at Scale

At Suja, we weren't launching 275 SKUs because we were reckless. We were launching them because we had built a manufacturing advantage that let us test ideas faster than anyone else in the category.

When Trader Joe's spotted the celery juice trend in 2019 - after a feature on The Rachael Ray Show - they called us. Trader Joe's typically prefers private label, and that process would have taken months. We went from the first call to product on shelves nationwide in less than six weeks.

That's not a co-manufacturer doing you a favor. That's what happens when you own your production and treat innovation as a core competency.

For context: when Coca-Cola invested in Suja in 2015, they had a framework called "Stages and Gates" for bringing new products to market. Their process typically took 16 to 18 months. We could take a new juice idea from concept to shelf in six weeks. When Coke wrote us a check for $90 million (part of a $150 million round valuing Suja at $300 million), that speed was a significant part of what they were buying.

But the key isn't just the speed. It's the discipline on the back end. We launched constantly, and we cut constantly. The 220 SKUs we discontinued weren't failures - they were data. They taught us what our consumer wanted and what they didn't.

---

## Innovation Is Not Churn If It Improves Turns

I eventually had an answer for my board member, though it took a while to articulate clearly: innovation is not churn if it improves your turns and strengthens your economics.

The test isn't "how many SKUs are we launching?" The test is: "Are the SKUs we're keeping performing above the category median? Are they generating the velocity our retail partners need? And are we cutting underperformers before they cost us our shelf space?"

Here are the velocity benchmarks we used at Suja - and still think about today:

- **80%+ of category median:** You're winning. You can expand off-cycle, pitch new doors, negotiate from strength.
- **60-80% of median:** You're probably fine for the next reset. Keep executing.
- **50%:** Acceptable. But watch it closely.
- **30-50%:** You need a clear plan to improve, or you won't survive the next reset.
- **Below 20%:** Something is seriously wrong. Cut it or fix it immediately.

Most founders know their volume. They know their revenue. But they're not benchmarking against the category median on a per-store, per-week basis. That's a problem. Because your retailer absolutely is.

Retailers give you roughly nine months after placement to prove you belong on shelf. In highly competitive categories, sometimes less. That clock is running from day one, and the only metric that matters is turns.

## The Decision That Moved Our Margin 800 Basis Points

The clearest example I can give you of smart portfolio management happened around 2018-2019, right after Coca-Cola decided not to move forward with acquiring the rest of Suja.

We were sitting at roughly $100 million in revenue with about 32% gross margin. We were burning more than $10 million a year. The situation was serious, and we had to make hard choices about the portfolio.

Two products were up for evaluation: kombucha and wellness shots.

Kombucha was... okay. We'd actually solved one of the hardest technical challenges in the category - maintaining consistent alcohol levels. The product was technically solid. But consumers didn't see Suja as a kombucha brand. We'd outsourced production. And our gross margin on kombucha was sitting around 12%.

Wellness shots were a different story. We produced them internally. Gross margin was closer to 60%. At Costco, they were selling about $2,000 per week per club in a head-to-head test against competitors. We'd entered the category as the third player, behind VIVE and KOR, and captured 30%+ market share within a year.

We cut the kombucha. We leaned hard into shots.

That single product mix decision - not a revenue initiative, not a marketing campaign, just a portfolio choice - moved our company-wide gross margin from about 32% to about 40%. Without growing revenue at all.

"Gross margin determines destiny." I mean that literally. Swapping 12% margin products for 60% margin products changes the entire trajectory of your business.

## A Simple Framework for Every SKU Decision

Here's how I think about it now:

**Launch when:**
- You're building on what you already do well. Expansion strengthens a brand when it deepens your core identity. Random expansion weakens it.
- You have a retail partner willing to test it. Even a 13-week Costco rotation is real market data.
- You can afford to fail and learn. Financially and operationally.
- You have a Plan B ready if Plan A doesn't perform. At least half the time, Plan A doesn't materialize.

**Hold when:**
- Velocity is between 50-80% of category median and trending upward.
- You have a clear path to improving turns: a promotional event, a packaging refresh, a new channel.
- The SKU plays a strategic role in a retail relationship even if the economics aren't perfect yet.

**Kill when:**
- Velocity has been below 30% of category median through two consecutive resets with no improvement.
- The margin profile doesn't support your business model.
- Consumers don't associate the product with what your brand stands for.
- You're keeping it because of sunk cost, not because of consumer data.

That last one is the real danger. The sunk cost fallacy in CPG is brutal. Founders pour $200K into a co-packer run, then keep funding a slow-moving SKU for two more years because they can't admit the initial decision was wrong. Cut your losses early. Redirect that capital into your heroes.

---

## The Cadence Matters as Much as the Decision

You don't need to be launching a new SKU every nine days to manage your portfolio well. That pace was specific to our manufacturing advantage and our market timing at Suja. Most early-stage founders should be launching fewer products - but doing it more deliberately.

What matters is the cadence of learning. Every launch should teach you something. Every discontinuation should reveal something about your consumer you didn't know before. If you're launching products and not measuring their performance against the category baseline within 90 days, you're not innovating. You're gambling.

And don't confuse distribution gains with velocity gains. Getting a new SKU placed in 500 stores is not the same thing as proving it belongs on shelf. The only number that matters to the buyer is turns per store per week. Everything else is noise.

CPG is a "penny profit" business. The pennies matter. A SKU that does 60% of category median in 300 stores is a better business asset than a SKU doing 25% of median in 1,000 stores. More doors does not solve a velocity problem. It amplifies it.

---

## The Bottom Line

My board member wasn't entirely wrong. We did have too many SKUs at certain points. We did create operational complexity we didn't always need. But the underlying habit - launch, learn, cut, redeploy - is the right one.

The brands I see struggling with portfolio management are almost always keeping too much alive. They're funding mediocre products instead of doubling down on winners. They're adding SKUs instead of adding velocity behind the one or two products that are actually resonating.

Your best product can carry your whole brand. One SKU that drives 30%+ more turns than everything else in your lineup is worth more than 15 average performers. Find that product. Kill what's distracting from it. And build everything around it.

The wellness shot that became one of Suja's most valuable products wasn't something we planned. It emerged from a structured process of testing, measuring, and being willing to bet on the data rather than our instincts.

Innovation is not about having all the answers. It's about staying in the game long enough to find them.

Dream boldly. Plan soberly.
