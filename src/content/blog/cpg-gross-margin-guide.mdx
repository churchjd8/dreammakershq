If there's one number that determines whether your CPG brand will survive, scale, or die, it's gross margin. Not revenue. Not door count. Not Instagram followers. Gross margin.

Gross margin is the money left over after you pay for the product itself - ingredients, packaging, co-manufacturing, and freight to your warehouse. It's the money that funds everything else: your team, your marketing, your trade spend, your growth. If your gross margin is too thin, nothing else works. You can't afford to promote. You can't afford to hire. You can't afford to raise at a valuation that doesn't destroy your equity.

Here's how to think about gross margin as a CPG founder - and how to get it to where it needs to be.

---

## How to Calculate Gross Margin Correctly

This seems simple, but I see founders get it wrong constantly. Here's the formula:

**Gross Margin = (Net Revenue - COGS) / Net Revenue**

The key word is **net** revenue. That means revenue after trade spend, retailer deductions, and any other contra-revenue items. If you're calculating gross margin on gross revenue, you're overstating your margins and making decisions based on the wrong number.

**What goes into COGS:**
- Ingredients and raw materials
- Packaging (bottles, cans, labels, cases, shrink wrap)
- Co-manufacturing fees
- Inbound freight (from co-man to your warehouse)

**What does NOT go into COGS:**
- Outbound freight (to retailers/distributors) - this goes in operating expenses
- Trade spend - this is contra-revenue
- Slotting fees - operating expense
- Broker commissions - operating expense

Getting your chart of accounts right from day one saves you enormous headaches later. When investors look at your financials, they expect to see a CPG-standard income statement. If freight is in your COGS, your gross margin looks artificially low and you're not comparing apples to apples with industry benchmarks.

---

## The Benchmarks You Need to Hit

**Year 1: 40% gross margin (minimum).** At 40%, you have enough room to fund basic trade spend (15-20% of net revenue), cover your operating expenses, and still have a path to profitability. Below 40%, the math gets very tight very fast.

**Year 3: 50% gross margin (target).** As you scale, your COGS should come down through volume-based pricing from co-mans, better ingredient sourcing, and packaging optimization. The best CPG brands operate at 50-60% gross margin.

**Below 35%: Danger zone.** At this level, after trade spend and operating expenses, you're almost certainly burning cash faster than you should be. Fix your margin structure before you scale.

## 7 Levers to Improve Your Gross Margin

### 1. Renegotiate co-man pricing

As your volume increases, you have leverage. Most co-man contracts have volume-based pricing tiers. If you've outgrown your current tier, renegotiate. If your co-man won't move on pricing, start conversations with alternatives. Competition keeps everyone honest.

### 2. Switch co-manufacturers

The co-man that was right for your first 10,000 cases is probably not right for your first 100,000 cases. Larger co-mans offer better per-unit economics at scale. Plan to switch co-mans 3-5 times from launch to full scale.

### 3. Optimize your packaging

This is one of the highest-leverage moves you can make. I worked with a brand that switched from sleeved cans to printed cans and saved $2.40 per case. The consumer didn't notice. The founder noticed immediately. Look at every component of your packaging and ask: is there a less expensive option that doesn't compromise the consumer experience?

### 4. Source ingredients directly

If your co-man is sourcing ingredients for you, they're marking them up 10-20%. That's fair in the early stages when you don't have purchasing relationships. But as you scale, start sourcing key ingredients directly. The savings go straight to your gross margin.

### 5. Reduce waste and overruns

Talk to your co-man about waste rates. Industry standard is 2-3%, but some co-mans run higher. Every percentage point of waste is product you paid for but can't sell.

### 6. Right-size your SKU count

More SKUs means more complexity, more changeovers, and higher per-unit costs. If a SKU isn't pulling its weight in velocity, consider cutting it. The margin improvement from simplifying your portfolio can be significant.

### 7. Raise your price

This is the lever founders are most afraid to pull, and often the most impactful. If your product is genuinely differentiated and your velocity is strong, a 5-10% price increase may have minimal impact on volume but a meaningful impact on margin. Test it in a few accounts before rolling it out broadly.

---

## The Bottom Line

Gross margin isn't just a financial metric - it's a strategic one. It determines how much you can invest in growth, how attractive you are to investors, and ultimately whether your brand is built to last. Get it right early, and everything else gets easier.
