Three slides in, and I already knew we were in trouble.

It was 2014. We were pitching a roomful of institutional investors for what would become one of Suja's larger rounds. Our deck was solid... or so I thought. We had the vision, the momentum, the revenue trajectory. Gone from $600K in 2012 to $18M in 2013 to $44M in 2014. The story practically told itself.

Then one of the investors stopped us on slide four.

"What's your velocity at Whole Foods versus the category median?"

Dead air.

Not because we didn't have the data. We did. We just hadn't included it. We'd built a deck like a tech company: market size, vision, team, financials. Standard structure. But CPG investors aren't tech investors. They think differently. They ask differently. And if your deck doesn't speak their language, you're already fighting uphill before you've said a word.

I've done 44 fundraising rounds over 30 years. $200 million raised. Eleven rounds in seven years at Suja alone. I've seen exactly what kills a CPG pitch. And it's usually not the product.

It's the deck.

## The First Thing Investors Actually Do with Your Deck

They flip to the financials. I know founders hate hearing that, but it's true. Most serious CPG investors — angels who've done this before, family offices, institutional shops — go straight to the financial model and the gross margin line. Not because they don't care about the brand. They do. But gross margin tells them whether this is a real business or a passion project.

Gross margin determines destiny. I mean that literally.

If you're showing 28% gross margin in your model, you haven't built a business. You've built a very expensive hobby. Institutional investors want to see a clear path to 40% or better. The best-positioned companies are already at 40% and showing the road to 50%. Below 40% with no improvement story is a hard stop for most institutional money.

Before you send a single deck, know your margins cold.

## The 10 Slides That Actually Matter

Keep the whole deck under 25 slides. Here's what CPG investors actually want to see, and roughly in this order:

**1. The hook.** One sentence. What is this thing? Who buys it? Why now? "The fastest-growing organic cold-pressed juice brand in America" is better than "revolutionizing the wellness category." Clarity beats cleverness. If you can have both, great.

**2. The market opportunity.** Use nested layers: industry, category, segment, niche. You don't need to own the ocean. You just need to own a well-defined pool. Give actual numbers with CAGRs. A niche growing 20% annually is a signal. A niche growing 2% is a warning sign.

**3. The problem and your solution.** What job are you doing for the consumer? Ground this in real behavior, not aspirational language. "Health-conscious consumers" is not an audience. That's a placeholder. Be specific enough that someone could draw a picture of your buyer.

**4. Proof of traction.** This is where most CPG decks fall apart. Don't just show revenue trajectory. Show velocity data. Velocity per store per week against the category median. Repeat rate (target 40-50%). ACV percentage. If you're at or above the 50th percentile on velocity and your repeat rate clears 40%, you have a business worth investing in. If repeat is below 20%... no deck saves you. You don't have loyal customers. You have trial.

You can market your way into trial. You cannot market your way into loyalty. That's the hard truth this slide has to address.

**5. Gross margin and unit economics.** Show your full COGS breakdown: raw materials, co-man rate, freight, packaging, spoilage allowance, retailer margin. Then show your landed cost per unit and what falls through. Then show the improvement roadmap. Some founders hide here. Don't. Sophisticated investors find it anyway. Finding it themselves kills trust faster than anything.

**6. Channel strategy.** Where are you today, and where are you going in what order? Distribution sequencing tells investors you won't blow yourself up chasing doors before you've built velocity. Don't confuse distribution gains with velocity gains. They are not the same thing. A retailer stocking you in 3,000 stores doesn't mean consumers are buying you in 3,000 stores. Show me you understand the difference.

**7. Competition and positioning.** Not a 2x2 grid with you in the top-right corner of "high quality / low price." Be honest. Show the category map. Identify real white space. Tell me what you do that the others cannot easily copy. Products are copyable. Brands are not. That's the answer investors are looking for here.

**8. Team.** Show me the operator, not just the visionary. Investors want to know who's waking up every morning figuring out how to get 40,000 cases through a co-man by Thursday. The team slide isn't a resume page. It's a confidence slide. Great early-stage investors back people before they back plans.

**9. The financial model.** Bottom-up only. I cannot stress this enough. Don't project "capturing 1% of a $10 billion market." Nobody believes that. Nobody. Build from real assumptions: door count by account, velocity per store per week, repeat rate, pricing by channel, COGS assumptions layered out quarter by quarter, opex tied to actual headcount. A credible bottom-up model says you understand the mechanics of your own business. A top-down model says you don't.

**10. The ask.** How much, at what valuation, for what milestones, on what timeline. Target 18 months of runway minimum. Here are the rough valuation benchmarks I've used: pre-revenue pre-money typically falls between $1M and $5M; early retail brands with initial traction $4M to $10M; emerging CPG with $3M to $20M in trailing revenue, expect 3 to 4x trailing twelve-month net sales. Push valuation too far, and you invite a down round. Down rounds are highly dilutive and damaging to credibility. Both are recoverable. Neither is fun.

## What Kills Deals Before the Meeting Ends

Three things I've watched kill pitches that should have worked:

**A top-down financial model with no grounding in real unit economics.** Investors see through it immediately. It signals you don't understand how CPG actually works. CPG is a "penny profit" business. The pennies matter. Show me you know that.

**A gross margin you can't defend.** If your co-man is delivering 32% gross margin and you can't tell me how you get to 40%... I'm out. Revenue without margin is ego. That's not a philosophy. That's arithmetic.

**Desperation.** Enthusiasm attracts. Desperation repels. If you're raising from a position of weakness — cash under 90 days, SKUs getting cut from shelves, velocity declining — those problems show up before you even sit down. I've never successfully sold or financed a company from a position of weakness. The time to raise is when you don't desperately need it. Start the process at 18 months of runway. Treat 12 months as the floor, not the starting line.

## One More Thing About the Deck Itself

Lead with the deck in early conversations. Share the detailed financial model only after there's demonstrated serious interest. Keep it under 25 slides. And remember what the deck's only job is:

Get you the next conversation. That's it.

A pitch deck doesn't fund your company. It earns you the meeting where your story, your conviction, and your numbers can do the real work. Build it accordingly.

Dream boldly. Plan soberly. And when someone in that room asks about your velocity against the category median, have the answer ready.
