A senior Target buyer pulled me aside after dinner one night. We were at our house in San Diego, Suja was scaling fast, and we'd just announced we were going into Walmart. She looked at me and said: "Jeff, if you use the same barcode and the same pack size at Walmart that you use here, we're going to price-match them. You'll kill your margin at Target and you'll lose the account."

I had no idea.

We fixed it immediately. Launched a 10.5-ounce bottle at Walmart. Kept the 12-ounce at Target. The pricing held. Target stayed. Walmart grew into one of our biggest customers.

That one conversation probably saved us several million dollars in margin erosion. But the deeper lesson wasn't about barcodes. It was about something most founders never think about until it's too late: **the order you enter retail channels matters as much as which channels you enter.**

Retail strategy is not just about doors. It is about order.

## Most Founders Think About "More" When They Should Think About "Next"

When I talk to founders who've gotten their first big retail win, the instinct is always the same. Whole Foods said yes? Great, now go get Kroger. Kroger picked you up? Go to Target. Target's onboard? Go to Walmart. More doors, more revenue, more growth.

That logic is seductive. And it's wrong.

Here's the problem: every channel has a different velocity expectation, a different price architecture, a different promotional cadence, and a different kind of consumer. When you enter a channel before your brand is ready for it, you don't just underperform. You contaminate everything that follows.

Don't confuse distribution gains with velocity gains. Distribution gains without the velocity to back them up is just a more expensive way to fail.

I've watched brands get Walmart placement six months after launch, have a disastrous first year on shelf, get discontinued, and then try to get back into Whole Foods... and find the door closed. Because the natural channel didn't want a brand that had already failed at mass.

Sequencing matters because credibility compounds. Get it right and each channel gives you leverage in the next. Get it wrong and the whole thing unravels in the wrong order.

## The Sequencing Framework That Actually Works

Here's how I think about it. Not as gospel, but as a map built from watching a lot of brands succeed and fail.

**Stage 1: Natural and Specialty (Whole Foods, Sprouts, Fresh Market, Natural Grocers)**

This is where you earn the right to exist. The natural channel consumer is forgiving, vocal, and highly loyal. They'll try your product, tell their friends, and post about it. If your product doesn't resonate here... it's not going to resonate anywhere.

More importantly, the natural channel is where you build velocity data. You need to hit at least the 50th percentile of category velocity before you go anywhere else. Ideally 60-70%. If you're at 30%, you have a product problem, not a distribution problem. More doors won't fix it.

Do not evaluate Whole Foods purely by traditional profit and loss metrics. It is also a marketing platform. It signals to every other retailer that you are legit.

**Stage 2: Regional Conventional (Kroger, Publix, Albertsons, HEB)**

Once your velocity story is clean and your gross margins are north of 40%, you're ready to go broader. Regional conventional is where your brand either proves it can travel beyond the core consumer... or doesn't.

This stage tests your promotional discipline. Conventional grocery will ask for trade spend, scan deals, and TPR (temporary price reductions) from day one. If you haven't done the financial modeling on what your velocity has to be to justify those promotions, you will bleed out slowly.

At Rowdy Energy, we paid nearly $1 million in one-time slotting fees to Albertsons. Velocity was well below hurdle levels. They discontinued us after 8 months. The money was gone and so was the account. Painful lesson. The product and the timing weren't right for that channel yet.

Know your velocity hurdle before you write that check.

**Stage 3: Mass (Target, then Walmart)**

This is where scale happens. But it comes with a price architecture that can destroy the margins you've spent years building if you're not careful.

I'd sequence Target before Walmart nearly every time. Target's consumer skews toward better-for-you, natural, premium. They'll pay more. They'll repeat. They're closer to your natural channel consumer than Walmart is.

Walmart operates on EDLP, everyday low price. That model creates pricing tension across your entire portfolio. Once Walmart has your product, every other retailer sees your shelf price. If you haven't protected your pricing architecture with differentiated pack sizes, different barcodes, or channel-specific SKUs, you will be fighting price-matching fires for years.

We built strong presence at Whole Foods, Target, and Costco before we went deep into Walmart. Not because Walmart was unimportant... but because by the time we got there, we had brand equity, velocity data, and enough leverage to negotiate a structure that protected us.

**Stage 4: Club (Costco, Sam's Club)**

Club is its own world. A Costco buyer expects around $1,000 per club per week. Most grocery stores see $15 per store per week on a good brand. The expectation isn't 10x. It's 60x.

Club is also a treasure hunt. Costco's 13-week rotation model means you're not getting a year to prove yourself. You're getting a quarter. If you can't generate that kind of velocity in that kind of window, club chews you up and spits you out.

What Costco will do for you, if you win... is remarkable. We ran head-to-head tests at Costco on wellness shots against a competitor. We took 30-plus percent market share within a year. The volume is enormous. But you earn club, you don't get discovered there.

## The Two Things That Kill Sequence Discipline

**1. Cash pressure makes founders irrational.**

I've seen it dozens of times. The business is burning more than it's producing. A buyer from a channel you're not ready for calls with an offer. The math doesn't work but the optics feel good. You say yes.

Hope is not a strategy. A big retail account at the wrong time is often a liability disguised as an opportunity. If the velocity isn't there, the account will disappear in 9 months and you'll have burned the slotting fees, the promotional budget, and your credibility in that channel.

**2. Brokers who care about commissions more than sequencing.**

Not all brokers, but some. A broker who gets paid on placements has a different incentive than a broker who gets paid on performance. Ask them directly: "What channel sequence do you recommend and why?" If they don't have a clear answer, or if the answer is "everywhere at once," find a different broker.

The right broker will tell you when you're not ready for an account. The wrong one will get you in too early and leave you to clean up the mess.

## The Number That Should Drive Every Sequencing Decision

Velocity. Units sold per store per week. That's the metric.

At 80-plus percent of category median, you reign supreme. You can negotiate. You can expand. You have leverage.

At 50 percent, you're doing okay but you're not yet in a position to push into harder channels.

Below 30 percent, something is wrong. Fix it before you go anywhere.

I don't care how many doors a brand has if the velocity is low. 5,000 doors selling two units per store per week is not a business. It's a distribution map that looks impressive until the next reset.

Build velocity first. Then buy doors.

## One More Thing

There is no universally correct sequence. If your brand is born in conventional grocery because that's where your category lives, start there. If your core consumer shops at Costco, test that first. The framework I've laid out works for most better-for-you consumer brands in the US market. Use it as a starting point, not a straitjacket.

But the underlying principle never changes: **earn the right to each channel before you enter it.** Go when you can win, not when an opportunity shows up.

Retailers reward measurable growth inside their four walls. They don't give you more space because you asked nicely. They give you more space because your velocity earned it.

Get the sequence right and every channel you enter makes the next one easier.

Get it wrong and you'll spend more time defending the decisions you made than building the brand you envisioned.

Dream boldly. Plan soberly.
