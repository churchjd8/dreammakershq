A few years back, I was co-founder of Rowdy Energy -- Kyle Busch's brand, energy drinks. We were executing, building velocity, making some noise. Then we got the call. Albertsons wanted us.

We took it. Paid nearly a million dollars in one-time slotting fees to get into a major Albertsons region. The team was excited. I was cautious -- we didn't have the velocity data to back it up. We weren't sure the brand story translated to that consumer in that channel.

Eight months later, Albertsons discontinued us.

That's not a cautionary tale about Albertsons. It's a cautionary tale about sequencing.

---

## Why Conventional Grocery Is Different

Conventional grocery is the holy grail for most CPG founders. Kroger. Albertsons. Safeway. Publix. HEB. These are the stores where America shops. 2,700 Kroger locations. Over 2,200 Albertsons. The numbers sound incredible when you're pitching investors or telling your story at a dinner party.

But here's the thing: the doors don't make you. The velocity does.

Whole Foods buyers and conventional grocery buyers are different animals. Whole Foods leans into discovery. They'll take a bet on an emerging brand with a compelling story and category-expanding potential. They're category evangelists, in a lot of ways.

Kroger, Albertsons, Safeway... these are category optimizers. Their job is to maximize turns per linear foot of shelf. Every SKU they carry has to justify its space by outselling the alternative they could put there instead.

When a conventional buyer sits across from you, they are asking one question: "What's your velocity, and can you prove it?" They're not as interested in your brand story. They're not interested in your Instagram following. They want scan data -- velocity per store per week at comparable retailers. And if you don't have a compelling answer, the meeting is over before it starts.

Don't confuse distribution gains with velocity gains. This is one of the most expensive mistakes in CPG.

---

## The Right Sequence

Here's the order I've followed -- and advised -- across eight companies:

**Natural specialty first.** Whole Foods, Fresh Market, Sprouts. These retailers have lower slotting requirements (sometimes none), more discovery-oriented buyers, and consumers who index highest for new premium products. They're your proving ground. You're learning the business on a more forgiving stage.

**Then Target and regional naturals.** You're building ACV, you're proving velocity in a broader channel, and you're collecting scan data that tells a real story.

**Then Costco, if you have the right pack configuration.** Costco's expectations are in a different league entirely -- they want roughly $1,000 per club per week versus $15 per store per week in a typical grocery environment. But if you can win there, it's an enormous signal. At Suja, our wellness shots were doing $2,000 per week per club. That kind of velocity opens every door downstream.

**Conventional grocery comes later.** After you've built the proof of concept that Kroger's buyer actually needs to say yes.

Walk into conventional too early and you're paying millions in slotting fees to educate a channel that isn't ready for you. Walk in at the right time with the right data, and the conversation changes entirely. I've watched both happen. The difference isn't the product. It's the timing.

---

## Run the Slotting Math Honestly

Most founders don't. Here's how.

Slotting fees in conventional grocery typically run $5,000-$15,000 per SKU per region. If you're launching into a Kroger region with three SKUs, you're looking at $45,000-$135,000 just to get on shelf. Before your first sale.

Treat that like customer acquisition cost. If your average contribution per unit after trade spend and freight is $1.25, and you paid $90,000 in slotting to enter a 300-store region... you need to sell 72,000 units just to break even on the placement. At three units per store per week, that's 80 weeks. Nearly two years.

Does your velocity projection support that math? Most founders never ask.

The brands that get hurt in conventional aren't always the ones who failed to negotiate slotting. They're the ones who never modeled what slotting costs on a fully-loaded basis -- and then ran out of working capital trying to survive a placement that was structurally upside down from day one.

"Revenue without margin is ego." Applies to doors without velocity too.

## Know Your Velocity Benchmarks

Once you're on shelf, this is the framework I use to evaluate whether a placement is working:

- **80%+ of category median:** You're winning. Push for expansion at the next reset.
- **60-80%:** You're okay. Protect the placement, work on velocity drivers.
- **50%:** Holding steady, but not safe. Don't add doors -- improve turns.
- **30-50%:** You need a plan fast. Talk to your broker. Talk to the buyer before they talk to you.
- **Below 20%:** Something is fundamentally broken -- product, price, placement, or all three.

Most retailers build planograms for roughly 12 months with a 6-month refresh. Once you're on shelf, you have about 9 months to prove you belong. In highly competitive categories... sometimes less.

If you're not tracking velocity weekly and comparing to category benchmarks, you're flying blind in a channel with zero tolerance for underperformers. Jody Cnossen, who ran Kroger and Publix for us at Suja, had a line she used constantly: "The truth of the business is at the shelf." She was right. The shelf doesn't lie.

---

## How to Actually Win

The brands that succeed at conventional grocery do a few things consistently.

**They come in with proof, not promises.** Real scan data from natural specialty. Repeat rate above 40%. Velocity at or above category median. Buyers don't want a pitch -- they want evidence. The best retail meetings at Suja were the ones where Jody barely had to say anything because the data did the work.

**They protect pricing integrity across channels.** A senior Target buyer gave us critical advice early on at Suja: don't use identical barcodes and pack sizes across all retailers. If you do, Target will price-match Walmart's EDLP and you lose your premium positioning overnight. We launched a 10.5-oz bottle at Walmart and a 12-oz at Target. Small operational headache. Massive strategic win. Lauren Soward managed that across Target and Walmart for us without adding a single headcount. It matters.

**They invest in velocity before they invest in doors.** More stores is not always better. A hundred stores with strong velocity builds the data story you need to expand. A thousand stores with weak velocity drains your trade spend budget and puts every buyer's confidence in your brand at risk. "Gross margin determines destiny" -- and so does velocity, because velocity is what earns you the right to grow.

**They help the buyer win the category.** Conventional buyers are measured on category performance, not your brand's survival. If you walk in with category data that shows a format gap, a pricing hole, or a consumer trend they're missing -- you become a partner, not a petitioner. Buyers who see you as a category partner open more doors than buyers who see you as another emerging brand looking for shelf space.

---

## The Bottom Line

Conventional grocery will test everything about your business -- your margins, your velocity, your operational discipline, your trade spend management. It is not forgiving. It is not patient. And it will not bail you out if your unit economics aren't there when you walk in.

But for a CPG brand with the right proof of concept and the right timing? It is one of the most powerful growth engines in consumer goods.

I made the mistake at Rowdy Energy -- went in too early, paid the price. I've watched dozens of other founders make the same call. The call always feels justified in the moment. The doors, the logos, the story you get to tell...

Get your natural specialty right first. Build your velocity proof. Run the slotting math honestly. And when you walk into that Kroger buyer meeting, let the numbers do the talking.

Distribution is not strategy. Velocity is.
