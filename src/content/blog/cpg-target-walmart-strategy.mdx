The challah bread had the Coca-Cola and Suja logos baked right into it.

My mother-in-law Rachel made it for a dinner Linda and I hosted at our home for the Target buying team. We pushed the conference room aside -- literally -- and replaced it with a home-cooked meal, a kitchen table, and real conversation. Good wine. Stories. The kind of evening where you stop being a vendor and start being a partner.

That Target buyer still talks about that dinner.

I tell that story because a lot of CPG founders think winning at Target and Walmart is about having the right product at the right price. And yes, that matters. But the brands that actually last in those channels understand something deeper: these aren't just bigger grocery stores with harder math. They're fundamentally different businesses with different cultures, different economics, and a completely different relationship with the brands on their shelves.

Get the relationship wrong and all the velocity data in the world won't save you.

---

## Why Mass Retail Is a Different Game

Most founders approach Target and Walmart the same way they approach Whole Foods -- just scaled up. More stores, bigger numbers, higher stakes. They go in with their brand story, their hero SKU, their pitch on why the consumer needs them.

And then they learn the hard way.

Whole Foods is a category evangelist. They take bets on emerging brands. They see themselves as scouts -- finding the next thing before the consumer even knows to ask for it. A compelling brand story, a growing category trend, a unique positioning... that gets you a meeting, and sometimes a yes.

Target and Walmart are category optimizers. Their job is to maximize turns per linear foot of shelf space. Full stop. When you sit across from a Target buyer, they're running one calculation: will this product drive more revenue and category growth than whatever I'm replacing it with? And when you sit across from a Walmart buyer, the question is even more elemental: can this survive EDLP pricing while still making economic sense for my store?

Your Instagram following doesn't answer that question. Your brand story barely registers. What registers is velocity -- units per store per week at comparable retailers -- and a business model that holds together under the pressure of their margin requirements.

"If the consumer is the holy grail, retailers hold the keys to the kingdom." And Target and Walmart hold different keys than anyone else.

---

## The Sequence Matters More Than the Destination

Here's the biggest mistake I see CPG founders make: they treat Walmart as the finish line. The prize. The sign that you've made it.

Walmart is not the finish line. Done at the wrong time, it's a trap.

At Suja, we followed a specific sequencing logic that I've now used and recommended across eight companies. Natural specialty first (Whole Foods, Sprouts, Fresh Market). Then Target and regional naturals to broaden ACV and collect real scan data. Then Costco, if you have the pack configuration and velocity to justify it -- Costco expects around $1,000 per club per week, compared to roughly $15 per store per week at a typical grocery account. Then conventional grocery. And Walmart comes at the back half of that build-out, once you have enough pricing power, brand equity, and operational scale to handle it.

Why does sequence matter? Because Walmart runs an Everyday Low Price model. Once you're in Walmart at a certain price point, that becomes the floor that every other retailer sees. Target will price-match Walmart's EDLP. Costco will notice. Even specialty retailers start to feel it.

A senior Target buyer gave me advice before we launched in Walmart that I've never forgotten: don't use identical barcodes or pack sizes across both retailers. We took it seriously. Suja launched a 10.5-ounce bottle at Walmart and a 12-ounce bottle at Target. Same great product, different configuration. Target got their pricing integrity. Walmart got their everyday price point. And we avoided the race to the bottom that kills emerging brands in mass retail.

Protect pricing integrity like it's a core asset. Because it is.

## The Walmart Trade

The call came in and Walmart wanted us to produce a plant-based smoothie -- Walmart exclusive, limited run, specific format that our production team... did not love. It wasn't our core product. The margins were unattractive. The operational complexity was real. My ops team was actively unhappy about it.

I said yes anyway.

Not because the smoothie was a great business on its own. It wasn't. But Walmart was willing to give us expanded distribution on our highest-performing SKUs in exchange. So we did one operationally inefficient run, absorbed the friction, and came out the other side with meaningfully more doors for the products that actually moved our business.

The calculated trade paid off. Walmart is still one of Suja's largest customers.

This is something most founders don't understand about mass retail: it's a relationship built over time through give and take. You don't just sell in. You earn your position -- by being reliable, by being willing to solve problems for the buyer that don't necessarily make you rich in the short term, by showing you understand their model and are invested in their success.

Help the retailer win the category. Not just push your product.

## Building the Relationship Like It's Actually a Relationship

Back to that Target dinner.

We hosted the buying team at our house. Home-cooked meal. Cocktails. Real conversation about what was happening in their category. We brought them to our manufacturing facility the next day. Someone suggested we all pile into an oversized bed for a photo. We did it. (Yes, there is a photo.) Real trust gets built through shared meals, shared stories, and genuine laughter... not Zoom calls and formatted presentations.

Retailers are human beings running difficult businesses with enormous pressure. They want to partner with brands they trust. Brands that bring them data proactively. Brands that come with a plan for underperforming SKUs instead of waiting to get cut. Brands that make them look good to their category managers.

At Suja, we developed a core list of principles for how we approached every retail relationship. Help them win the category. Understand their model better than your competitors. Play the long game. Reinforce your story relentlessly. Offer smart exclusives where it makes sense. Stay flexible. These aren't soft principles -- they're the operating system behind every meaningful retail partnership we ever built.

The brands that treat retail like a transaction... they get managed like transactions. One reset and you're out.

## The Velocity Reality Check

Mass retail is not where you go to discover whether your product works. It's where you go to prove that it already does.

If you're not clearing at least the 50th percentile of velocity versus your category competitors at your current retailers, you are not ready for Target. You are not ready for Walmart. Full stop. You need more time at the proving ground stage.

I paid nearly $1 million in slotting fees to get Rowdy Energy into Albertsons. Velocity came in significantly below hurdle levels. Eight months later, they discontinued us. That million dollars bought us nothing durable -- because we went in before the velocity story was real.

Don't confuse distribution gains with velocity gains. That phrase should live on your wall if you're anywhere near a major retail expansion decision.

Mass retail will find every weakness in your business. Pricing weakness. Velocity weakness. Operational weakness. Capital weakness. The floor drops faster than you expect, and the margin for error is tiny. CPG is a "penny profit" business, and nowhere is that more true than Walmart.

---

## What Mass Retail Readiness Actually Looks Like

Before you walk into a Target or Walmart meeting, honest answers to these questions:

Do you have scan data from at least 200-300 comparable doors showing consistent velocity above the category median? If not, not yet.

Is your gross margin at or above 40% after trade spend and freight? If not, the numbers at EDLP won't work.

Can you handle regional scale -- meaning, can you actually fill the orders without blowing up your supply chain or your cash position? Mass retail will expose operational gaps immediately.

Do you have a pricing architecture that can survive EDLP without blowing up your other channels? If not, you need that 10.5oz vs. 12oz conversation before you go in.

And finally... are you building a relationship, or just chasing doors? Because the buyers will know the answer before you finish your first slide.

"Retail strategy is not just about doors. It is about order."

Earn the right to mass retail. Get there at the right time, with the right data, with the right relationship already in motion. That challah bread wasn't just dinner. It was the investment that paid off for years.
