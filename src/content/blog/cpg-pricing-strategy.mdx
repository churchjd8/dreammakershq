Most CPG founders set their price one of two ways: they look at what competitors charge and match it, or they calculate their cost and add a margin they think sounds reasonable. Both approaches are wrong - or at least incomplete.

Pricing is one of the highest-leverage decisions in your business. Get it right, and you fund your growth, attract the right consumers, and build a brand that retailers want on their shelves. Get it wrong, and you're either leaving money on the table or pricing yourself into a margin hole you can't climb out of.

Here's how to think about pricing as a CPG founder.

---

## Start With Your Target Margin, Not Your Cost

Most founders start with cost and work forward: "My COGS is $X, so I need to charge $Y to make money." That's backwards.

Start with your target gross margin and work backward. In CPG, you need to be at 45-50% gross margin (excluding freight) by year two or three. If you can't get there, your business model has a structural problem that no amount of revenue growth will fix.

Here's the math:

- **Your target gross margin:** 50%
- **Your net revenue per unit** (after trade spend): This is what you actually collect
- **Your COGS per unit:** Ingredients + packaging + co-man fees

If your COGS is $1.50 per unit and you need 50% gross margin, your net revenue per unit needs to be at least $3.00. If trade spend is running 20% of gross revenue, your gross revenue per unit needs to be about $3.75. Work backward from there to your suggested retail price.

---

## Understand the Retail Math

Your retail price is not your revenue. There are several layers between what the consumer pays and what you actually collect:

**Suggested Retail Price (SRP)** - What the consumer sees on the shelf.

**Wholesale Price** - What you sell to the distributor, typically 40-50% below SRP. If your SRP is $4.99, your wholesale price to the distributor might be $2.50-$3.00.

**Net Revenue** - Your wholesale price minus trade spend (promotions, slotting, scan-backs). Trade spend typically runs 15-25% of gross revenue depending on the channel.

**Gross Profit** - Net revenue minus COGS. This is what you have left to fund everything else.

If you don't understand each of these layers, you'll set a retail price that looks good on the shelf but leaves you with no margin to operate.

---

## Price for the Consumer, Not for Yourself

Your price sends a signal. In CPG, price communicates quality, positioning, and who your product is for.

**Too low:** You signal that your product is a commodity. You attract price-sensitive consumers who will leave you the moment a cheaper option shows up. And you kill your margins.

**Too high:** You limit trial. Consumers won't take a $7.99 risk on a brand they've never heard of when there's a trusted option at $4.99. You need to earn the right to premium pricing through brand building and proof of quality.

**The sweet spot:** Price at or slightly above the category average for your channel. This positions you as premium without creating a trial barrier. As your brand grows and your velocity proves consumer demand, you can test price increases.

---

## The Promotion Trap

Here's something most founders don't realize: roughly 50% of your trade spend is wasted. Half of what you spend on promotions would have been bought by the consumer anyway at full price. The other half drives incremental trial and volume.

The problem is that most founders don't know which half is which. They run promotions because the retailer asks for them, or because they think discounting will drive velocity. But without measuring lift - the incremental volume a promotion generates above baseline - you're flying blind.

Before you run a promotion, know:

- **Your baseline velocity** (units per store per week without any promo)
- **The expected lift** from the promotional mechanic you're running
- **The cost of the promotion** versus the margin on the incremental units
- **Whether the promotion drives repeat purchase** or just one-time trial

I've seen brands where a 2-for-$5 promo outperformed a 2-for-$4 promo - because the shelf tag alone created purchase intent even though it was a shallower discount. The only way to know is to test and measure.

---

## When to Raise Your Price

Founders are terrified of raising prices. They think they'll lose velocity, upset retailers, and alienate consumers. In reality, a well-executed price increase is one of the most powerful moves you can make.

**Raise your price when:**

- Your velocity is strong and above category average
- Your repeat purchase rate is 30%+ (consumers love your product)
- Your gross margin is below 45% and you need room to operate
- Commodity costs have increased and the category is moving up
- You've built enough brand equity that consumers will pay more

**How to do it:**

- Test in a few accounts first before rolling out broadly
- Raise 5-10% at a time, not 25%
- Time it with a package refresh or new SKU launch so there's a reason
- Brief your retailer buyer in advance - don't surprise them
- Monitor velocity closely for 8-12 weeks after the increase

At Suja, we raised prices multiple times as the brand grew. Each time, there was nervousness internally. Each time, velocity held. Consumers who love your product will pay an extra 50 cents. The ones who won't weren't your core customer anyway.

---

## The DTC Pricing Advantage

If you're selling DTC (direct to consumer), you have a pricing advantage that most founders under-utilize. You're cutting out the distributor and the retailer, which means you keep a much larger share of each dollar.

Use DTC to:

- **Test price points** before rolling them out to retail
- **Sell at full margin** without trade spend eating into your revenue
- **Build a subscription base** at a slight discount (15-20% off retail) that still yields higher margin than wholesale
- **Launch new SKUs** and validate demand before committing to retail distribution

Your DTC price should generally be at or slightly below your retail SRP. Don't undercut your retail partners significantly - that creates channel conflict. But do use DTC as your highest-margin channel and your testing ground.

---

## The Bottom Line

Pricing isn't a one-time decision. It's a strategic lever that you should be actively managing throughout the life of your brand. Start with your target margin, understand the retail math, price for your consumer, measure your promotions, and don't be afraid to raise prices when you've earned the right to.

The founders who build $100M brands aren't the ones with the lowest prices. They're the ones who understand the relationship between price, margin, and brand value - and manage all three deliberately.
