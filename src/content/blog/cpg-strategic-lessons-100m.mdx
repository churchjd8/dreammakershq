Strategy in CPG isn't about PowerPoint decks. It's about making the right bets at the right time with limited resources... and being disciplined enough to stick with those bets when everyone around you wants you to chase something shiny.

Here are the nine strategic lessons that shaped how we built Suja Juice to $100M. I apply every one of them to the brands I work with today.

## 1. Validate Market Need Before You Spend

Before you lean in financially... before the big production run, before the national broker, before the expensive trade show booth... make sure there's a real market need for your product. Not a market want. A market need.

Too many founders fall in love with their product and skip this step. They assume that because they and their friends love it, the market will too. That's not validation. Validation is velocity data from real stores with real consumers making real purchase decisions with their own money.

Can you achieve 2x category average velocity in your initial retail accounts without heavy promotion? If yes, you've got something real. If no, go back and figure out why before you scale. Hope is not a strategy.

## 2. Balance Core Growth with Thoughtful Innovation

There's a tension every CPG founder faces: do you double down on your core product or innovate into new SKUs? The answer is both... but with discipline.

At Suja, we constantly got pulled toward shiny new product ideas. But we learned (sometimes the hard way) that reactive innovation... launching something because a retailer asked for it or because a competitor did it... almost always fails. Thoughtful innovation, driven by consumer insights and aligned with your brand DNA, is what works.

Your core product should be growing every quarter. If it's not, fix that before launching anything new. Innovation should amplify your core, not distract from it.

## 3. Timing Is Everything in Brand Development

You don't have to be the first brand in a category. But you can't be the 51st either.

There's a window in every category where consumer demand is building but supply hasn't caught up. That's where the magic happens. Suja didn't invent cold-pressed juice. But we hit the market at exactly the right moment... when consumers were demanding it but very few brands could deliver it at scale with national distribution.

Timing that window is part skill, part luck, and part obsessive attention to consumer trends. You can't control the luck, but you can control how closely you're watching.

## 4. Begin with the End in Mind

Before you build your brand, decide what success looks like. Are you building to sell? Building to hold? Building a platform company? Each path requires fundamentally different decisions about capitalization, team, growth rate, and margin structure.

I've seen founders raise venture capital for a brand they wanted to run forever. VC and "lifestyle business" don't mix (and if you don't understand why, we should talk). I've seen founders bootstrap when they should have raised because they didn't realize the category window was closing. Know your endgame. It shapes every decision you make along the way.

## 5. Embrace Chaos... It's Why You Exist

Big CPG companies are slow. They have committees, approval processes, and 18-month innovation cycles. That's your advantage. The chaos and speed of being a startup is what allows you to outmaneuver companies with 100x your resources.

Don't try to bring "big company process" into your startup too early. Yes, you need structure. But your ability to make a decision on Monday and execute by Friday is a superpower. Protect it. The moment you start needing three meetings and a deck to approve a decision, you've lost your edge.

## 6. Don't Confuse Distribution Gains with Velocity Gains

This is one of the most dangerous mistakes in CPG and I've talked about it a lot because it matters that much.

Your revenue is up 200%! Amazing, right? But wait... you went from 500 doors to 2,000 doors. Your per-door velocity actually dropped 15%. Revenue growth from distribution expansion masks the real health of your brand. Velocity per point of distribution is the metric that matters.

If your velocity is declining as you expand, you're building on sand. Eventually the retailers will see it too, and the resets will come.

Track revenue per store per week. Every week. If it's not growing or at least stable as you expand, stop expanding and fix the demand side.

## 7. Build Deep Retailer Relationships Fast

The ability to quickly establish collaborative relationships with retailers is a critical capability. Not transactional relationships where you're just another vendor with a line review. Collaborative relationships where you're a category partner.

This means sharing data proactively, presenting category insights (not just your brand story), investing in their shoppers, and making their category managers look like heroes to their bosses. That last part matters more than most founders realize. The brands that do this get more shelf space, better placement, and early access to opportunities that never hit the open market.

## 8. You Need Something Disruptive to Reach $100M

Incremental improvements don't build $100M brands. A slightly better chip. A somewhat healthier cookie. A marginally tastier drink. These might build $10M brands, but they won't break through at scale.

To reach $100M, you need genuine disruption. A new category. A new manufacturing process. A new consumer behavior. Something that makes people stop and say "this is fundamentally different." At Suja, it was bringing cold-pressed juice from a $12 niche product to an accessible, nationally distributed brand. That was disruptive. That changed the game.

## 9. Know When the Big Players Are Coming

When the largest companies in your category take notice, everything changes. Everything. If they want to acquire you, the window might be shorter than you think. If they decide to compete instead, you need to be prepared for a resource war you can't win on their terms.

At Suja, when Coca-Cola got involved, it changed the entire dynamic of our business. Understanding how to navigate relationships with the giants... whether they're potential acquirers, partners, or competitors... is a strategic skill that can make or break your brand's trajectory. When the largest beverage brand in the world is in the room, you better know what you're doing.
