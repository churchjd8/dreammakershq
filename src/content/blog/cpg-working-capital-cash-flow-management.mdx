The call came on July 3rd, 2018.

I had a house full of family getting ready for the Fourth. Kids running around. Linda in the kitchen. The kind of day you want to hold onto forever.

Scott Uzell called to tell me Coca-Cola would not be moving forward with acquiring the remaining portion of Suja.

Six weeks earlier, Coke's management and HR teams had visited our facility. We'd talked about integration. About the future. I'd let myself actually picture it.

I walked downstairs after that call and wept in front of my boys.

The next morning I woke up in Mayville, New York, to fireworks and a parade. Blue skies, 82 degrees, not a cloud in sight. And my stomach was somewhere near the floor.

Because here's what I was sitting with: $40 million in secured debt coming due that October. A company burning more than $10 million per year. Gross margins below 32%. And a $100 million revenue company that was, at certain moments, operating with less than $100,000 in the bank.

Read that again. Less than $100,000 in the bank. $100 million in annual revenue.

That's not a cash flow problem. That's an existential reckoning. And it taught me more about working capital than any MBA ever could.

---

## The Structural Problem Most Founders Don't See Coming

When you're early, working capital isn't really on your radar. You're thinking about product. About retail. About your next raise. You're thinking about the dream.

But CPG is a "penny profit" business. The pennies matter.

And the cash flow structure of this industry is brutal by design. You produce your product. You ship it to a distributor. You wait 30 to 60 days to get paid... sometimes longer. Meanwhile, you've already paid your co-man. You've already paid your raw material suppliers, your freight, your labor. Cash goes out fast. It comes back slow.

Layer in promotional spending -- which retailers deduct right off the top of your invoice -- and you've got a machine that burns cash at scale. The bigger you grow, the more it can hurt. Every new door you open, every new pallet you ship, you're financing it yourself while you wait to get paid.

Most founders think growth solves cash flow problems. It doesn't. It makes them bigger.

"Growth doesn't create strength. It reveals whether you ever truly had it in the first place."

---

## The Rule of Twos Will Find You Here

I talk about the Rule of Twos often. Whatever you think it's going to cost... double it. Whatever timeline you're working with... double that too.

Working capital is where the Rule of Twos shows up hardest.

The new retail partner you're excited about? They want 60-day payment terms. Your supplier? Net-30 or net-15. That gap in the middle is yours to finance. And this is before chargebacks. Before deductions. Before a pallet gets refused at the DC because a barcode is off by a digit.

I've watched sharp, capable founders -- people who had everything else figured out -- get taken out by working capital gaps they never saw coming. They weren't bad operators. They just weren't watching the right thing at the right time.

Hope is not a strategy. Hope is definitely not a cash flow model.

## What I Actually Did When We Had $100K in the Bank

After Coke's July 3rd call, my CFO Todd Fisher and I started living inside weekly cash projections. Not monthly. Weekly. We'd look at what was coming in, what was going out, and we'd make decisions accordingly.

That discipline is something most founders only build when they're forced to. Don't wait to be forced.

Here's what worked when we were staring at an empty account:

**Cut marketing before you cut operations.**

We cut marketing spend by more than $4 million. Revenue grew about 10% the following year. I'm still a little stunned by that number. But what it told me was this: strong brands with loyal customers don't always need massive spending to grow. If your product has real velocity, the shelf does the selling. Don't assume every marketing dollar is working as hard as you think.

"You can market your way into trial, but you cannot market your way into loyalty."

**Eliminate low-margin products ruthlessly.**

Our kombucha was outsourced at roughly 12% gross margin. Our wellness shots, produced internally, ran close to 60%. We swapped them. Company-wide gross margin moved from about 32% to 40% in a single product-mix shift. Not a cost cut. A margin decision.

Gross margin determines destiny. When you're cash-constrained, your highest-margin products are the ones keeping the lights on.

**Collect faster. Pay slower. Negotiate everything.**

Payment terms are negotiable. Every supplier knows this. Review your terms with top suppliers every six months. Adding 15 days to your payables can free up meaningful cash. Meanwhile, tighten your receivables. Know who owes you money and exactly when it lands.

**Use PO financing before personal guarantees.**

Purchase order financing sounds expensive -- it can run 20%+ annualized. But if it's never outstanding for more than 90 days, your effective cost is closer to 5%. That's real capital that doesn't put your house at risk.

I spent 40 years avoiding personal guarantees. I never signed one. The discipline that requires forced me to find creative financing solutions instead of the easy and dangerous route of pledging personal assets. Slow down any time a deal requires you to sign personally. There is almost always another path.

## The Numbers Every CPG Founder Should Know Cold

Maintain 12 to 18 months of cash runway. That's the target. Not six months. Not three. Twelve to eighteen.

Start your next fundraise when you have at least four months of runway remaining. Not two months. Not when you're desperate. Four months minimum -- because by the time you close a round and cash actually hits your account, you'll have burned through two of those months just running the process.

Monitor your burn rate weekly. Not monthly. Monthly review of burn rate is like checking your fuel gauge every hundred miles on a road you've never driven. By the time you see the problem, you're already stranded.

And here's the one I see founders skip most: run your P&L by account before you accept any new retail placement. Not every new door is worth opening. Some retailers pay slowly. Some require slotting fees that won't be recovered for 18 months. Some will demand trade spend that destroys your economics before you've proven velocity. Know the math before you shake hands.

At Rowdy Energy, we paid nearly $1 million in slotting fees to Albertsons. Velocity came in significantly below hurdle levels. Albertsons discontinued us after eight months. $1 million gone. That kind of mistake is only survivable if you've built enough runway to absorb it -- and if you've learned from it going forward.

## Working Capital as a Strategic Weapon

Here's what I want to push back on: the idea that working capital management is purely defensive. It's not. Used right, it's one of the most offensive tools you have.

At Walmart, we agreed to run an operationally messy, low-margin SKU -- a plant-based smoothie the production team hated -- in exchange for expanding distribution of our higher-performing SKUs. We understood exactly what we could absorb. We made the trade deliberately. Walmart remains one of Suja's largest customers today.

That decision required knowing your numbers well enough to play strategic chess. Founders who know their working capital position can make those calls. Founders who don't get played by buyers who understand the leverage better than they do.

"Retail strategy is not just about doors. It is about order."

The founders who win at retail long-term are the ones who can look a buyer in the eye and say with precision: "Here's what we can do. Here's what we can't. Here's what makes sense for both of us." That confidence doesn't come from intuition. It comes from clean numbers and weekly discipline.

---

## The Discipline That Saves You

When I look back at the Suja crisis -- $40 million due in October, less than $100K in the bank -- I don't dwell on what went wrong externally. I think about the internal discipline we built in order to survive it.

We became a different kind of company. Tighter. More intentional. Less optimistic in the dangerous way, more realistic in the productive way.

"I have never successfully sold or financed a company from a position of weakness."

That truth stings a little. The answer is never to wait until you're in the crisis. Build the discipline when things are good. Build it when you have margin for error, because someday you won't.

Revenue without margin is ego. And growth without cash discipline is just delayed pain.

Dream boldly. Plan soberly.

That's not a contradiction. That's the job.
