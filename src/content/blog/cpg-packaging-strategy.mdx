A founder I was advising a couple years back had one of the most beautiful brands I'd ever seen. I'm talking stop-you-in-the-aisle beautiful. Custom glass with a proprietary shape. Embossed labels. Matte finish with a spot gloss logo on the front panel. Every detail dialed in. First impression: this person gets it.

Second impression, once I looked at their financials: they really don't.

Their packaging costs were eating them alive. They were running at 28% gross margin with packaging that looked like it belonged on a 55% gross margin brand. The math was broken. And it was broken because they had made every single packaging decision from an aesthetic standpoint without ever asking what it was costing them per unit.

That's the trap. And it's one of the most expensive traps I see early-stage CPG founders fall into.

Your packaging has to do two completely different jobs at the same time. It has to stop a consumer in the aisle and earn the trial. And it has to fit inside a cost structure that leaves you enough margin to actually run a business. Most founders design for the first job and discover the hard way that they forgot about the second one.

---

## Packaging Has Three Jobs. Not One.

Before you brief a designer. Before you pick a format. Before you fall in love with a substrate. Understand that your packaging has three jobs. All three matter. Failing at any one of them is expensive.

**Job 1: Win the three-second scan.**

You have three seconds. Maybe less. A shopper is walking past a 48-foot gondola and something has to make them stop. Color. Shape. A clear benefit statement. Your brand name readable from four feet away. If your packaging doesn't pass the three-second test, your velocity numbers will tell you. And when velocity suffers, you're off shelf. The buyer doesn't have a long conversation with you about it. Your SKU just quietly disappears.

**Job 2: Justify the price at first touch.**

In natural and specialty retail especially, you're pricing at a significant premium to conventional. That premium has to feel earned before the consumer ever opens the product. Packaging communicates quality. The right label stock, the right finish, the right hierarchy of information... these things signal "worth it." And that's a repeat purchase decision being made at the shelf, not at the first taste.

You can market your way into trial, but you cannot market your way into loyalty. Packaging is a major driver of trial. Get it right.

**Job 3: Protect your margin.**

This is the one founders forget. Packaging is typically 15 to 25% of COGS for a beverage brand... and sometimes more. Every component decision is a margin decision. The format, the substrate, the label complexity, the print method, the case pack configuration... it all adds up. And those pennies matter. CPG is a "penny profit" business. The pennies matter.

## What This Looks Like in the Real Numbers

When I'm advising founders on pre-launch budgets, I budget roughly $40,000 for brand identity and packaging. That feels like a lot when you're bootstrapped and watching every dollar. But consider what that investment covers: the packaging design that will live on shelf for three to five years, every production run for the foreseeable future, every retailer presentation and sell-in deck, every consumer impression.

The bigger question isn't what you spend on design. It's what each component decision costs you every time you produce.

I worked with a beverage brand that switched from sleeved cans to printed cans. Same product. Same brand. Different production method. That switch saved them $2.40 per case. That doesn't sound like much until you do the math: at 50,000 cases per year, that's $120,000 back into your business. Annually. The consumer didn't notice. The P&L absolutely did.

That's the leverage inside packaging decisions. It's not glamorous. It's not a brand strategy conversation. It's just the work. And gross margin determines destiny, so you have to do the work.

## Match Your Format to Your Channel From the Start

Here's where a lot of founders make an expensive mistake. They design packaging for one channel and then try to shoehorn it into others. Or they design something that looks stunning in a design mockup but doesn't function on an actual retail shelf.

A few channel-specific principles that matter:

**Specialty and natural (Whole Foods, Sprouts, independents):** Consumers here are reading your label. They want provenance, ingredients, certifications, a clear point of view. Give them something worth reading. Premium finishes and materials play here because this shopper has made a values decision to be in this store. Your packaging should affirm that decision.

**Conventional grocery (Kroger, Safeway, Albertsons):** Clarity wins. These shoppers are moving faster with bigger baskets and less patience for small-print storytelling. Bright, bold, instantly scannable. You're competing with brands that have spent millions on shelf presence. Make the most important thing the most obvious thing.

**Club channel (Costco, Sam's):** Club packaging is its own world entirely. Multi-packs, different case quantities, different shelf depths and heights. Costco buyers want to see packaging specifically engineered for their environment... not your standard retail pack with a club sticker slapped on the back. If you're going to play in club, design for club from the beginning. I can't tell you how many founders get their Costco item test approved, rush to production, and realize their existing pack doesn't fit the warehouse format.

**E-commerce and DTC:** Your packaging has to survive shipping. I've seen brands with beautiful retail packaging that arrives to consumers dented, scuffed, or exploded in the box because nobody thought about what happens when a UPS driver throws the case from three feet up. If you're selling direct, the consumer's unboxing experience is your brand. Design your case pack for it.

## Don't Fall in Love With Your First Design

The packaging you launch with is not the packaging you'll scale with. That's fine. Plan for it.

Your first version should prove the concept, get you on shelf, and give you real consumer feedback. Then, as you hit volume milestones... as your co-man relationships mature and you're sourcing components at scale... that's when you start optimizing. Review your packaging COGS at every major production run milestone. Ask your co-man what components they're sourcing versus what you could source directly. Ask your label supplier whether digital or offset printing makes more sense at your current volume.

At Suja, we revisited packaging decisions repeatedly as we scaled. What made sense at 10,000 cases a month didn't make sense at 100,000. That's expected. The mistake is treating your first packaging economics as permanent. Nothing in CPG should be treated as permanent.

A related point: don't change your packaging in the middle of a fundraise. Investors get nervous when core brand elements are in flux. Nail the design, lock it in, then raise.

## A Few Rules I've Learned the Hard Way

**On format:** Match the format to the retail channel first, then optimize for margin. Don't start with the format that has the best COGS if it looks wrong on shelf. The margin optimization comes later. The shelf performance has to come first.

**On designers:** Pay for someone who understands retail. Not a logo designer. Not a generalist. Someone who has walked grocery aisles, understands planogram placement, and knows what "shoppable from four feet" actually means in practice. The difference between a $2,000 freelancer and a $15,000 packaging design studio that has gotten brands on Whole Foods shelves is not 7.5x the quality of the art. It's a package that functions vs. one that struggles.

**On complexity:** Every added design element is a potential COGS add. Foil stamping, embossing, multiple label panels, proprietary bottle shapes... all of it adds up. Add these things intentionally, not because they look cool in a mockup. Ask your co-man or packaging supplier what each element costs before you commit to it.

**On trademarks:** Your packaging design is a brand asset. File for trade dress protection on distinctive design elements early. I've seen founders get hit with IP disputes over packaging similarity that derailed their fundraising at exactly the wrong moment. Protect what you build.

**On overstocking for launch:** One of the most common early-stage mistakes is producing too much of your first run with packaging that hasn't been tested at retail. Start with a smaller pilot run. Get it on shelf. See how the packaging performs. Then commit to scale.

---

## The Principle Worth Remembering

Here's what I've come to believe after building eight companies and watching hundreds of CPG brands succeed and fail: packaging is the intersection of brand and economics. It's where your consumer story meets your unit cost. And it's one of the few places where a single decision... a format change, a label substrate upgrade, a printing method switch... can meaningfully move your gross margin without the consumer ever knowing.

Most founders treat packaging as a creative exercise. The ones who build durable businesses treat it as a financial lever.

Dream boldly when you design. Plan soberly when you cost it out. Your packaging is your best salesperson and your most negotiable COGS line item. Treat it like both.
