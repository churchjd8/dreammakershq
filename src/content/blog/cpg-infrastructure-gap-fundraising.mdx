I've sat across the table from hundreds of founders over the past thirty-five years. Some of them had incredible products. Category-defining brands, clean ingredients, beautiful packaging, the kind of thing that makes you say, "This should be on every shelf in America."

And a lot of them couldn't raise a dime.

Not because the product was wrong. Not because the pitch was bad. Because the foundation underneath the business wasn't built yet.

I call it the Infrastructure Gap. And it's the single biggest reason CPG startups don't get funded.

## The Misconception That Costs Founders Years

Here's what most founders believe: if the product is great and the market is big, the money will come. They pour everything into R&D, branding, maybe a few retail placements. They build a pitch deck with a massive TAM number and a hockey-stick revenue chart. Then they walk into investor meetings expecting to close.

And they get "interesting, but not right now."

They think the problem is the pitch. So they rework the deck. They think the problem is the product. So they reformulate, redesign, relaunch. They think they need more traction. So they go chase more doors.

None of that is the real issue.

The real issue is that when an investor asks, "Walk me through your unit economics by channel," they can't do it with precision. When the question is, "What's your bottoms-up path to 40% gross margin by year two?" — they don't have a model that answers it. When someone says, "Show me your cap table and dilution plan through Series A," they pull up a messy spreadsheet with guesses in it.

That's the Infrastructure Gap.

## What Infrastructure Actually Means

When I say infrastructure, I'm not talking about warehouses and production lines. Not yet. I'm talking about the operating foundation that proves your business is investable:

**Financial modeling.** Not a top-down forecast where you take the total market size and say you'll capture 2%. Investors see through that in about thirty seconds. I'm talking about a bottoms-up, SKU-by-SKU, account-by-account, channel-by-channel model that shows exactly how revenue builds. Monthly cash flow projections. Realistic assumptions. Sensitivity analysis for when things go wrong — because they will.

**Unit economics.** Your gross margin story needs to be airtight. What's your COGS breakdown — ingredients, packaging, co-man fees, freight, trade spend? Where are you today and what's the path to 45-50%? If you can't explain your margin architecture line by line, you're not ready.

**Cap table clarity.** Who owns what. What instruments are outstanding — SAFEs, convertible notes, equity? What does the dilution path look like through three or four rounds? Can you still retain 50%+ as founders at exit? If your cap table is a mess, sophisticated investors won't touch it.

**A real fundraising deck.** Ten to fifteen slides. Problem, solution, market, traction, business model, go-to-market, team, the ask. Not a brand book. Not forty slides of product photography. A tight, investor-grade presentation that answers the questions they're actually going to ask.

**Operational readiness.** Do you have your co-man agreements structured right? Do you understand your 3PL fee schedule? Is your freight categorized correctly on the P&L? These details might seem small, but the pennies matter. CPG is a penny-profit business. Always has been.

That's infrastructure. And most founders don't have it when they walk into their first investor meeting.

## Why This Gap Exists

It's not because founders are lazy or unintelligent. It's because nobody tells them this stuff.

The entrepreneurship world is full of advice about product-market fit, brand storytelling, social media growth. All of that matters. But nobody sits founders down and says, "Before you raise a single dollar, you need a five-year financial model built from the bottom up, a clean cap table, a dilution strategy, and unit economics you can defend under pressure."

That's not sexy. It's not what gets likes on social media. But it's what gets checks written.

I raised over $275 million across more than forty funding rounds in my career. At Suja, we went from $600K in revenue to over $100 million in five and a half years. We raised eleven rounds. And I can tell you — the rounds that went smoothly were the ones where we showed up with our infrastructure locked down. The ones that were painful? Those were the ones where we were scrambling to build the foundation while trying to close the deal.

Hope is not a forecasting model.

## The Gap in Action

Let me give you a few examples of what the Infrastructure Gap looks like in practice.

**The margin blind spot.** A founder comes to me with a product doing $2 million in revenue. Growing fast. Great velocity at the stores they're in. But when I dig into the numbers, their gross margin is 22%. They're losing money on every unit at scale and don't even realize it because they've never broken down their COGS line by line. They're running around celebrating revenue growth while the business bleeds cash.

Revenue without margin is ego.

**The top-down trap.** Another founder has a beautiful deck. The TAM slide says "The organic snack market is $47 billion." Their projections show capturing 0.5% of that in three years. Sounds reasonable, right? Except there's no bottoms-up logic behind it. No account-level assumptions. No velocity benchmarks. No channel strategy. An investor looks at that and sees a fantasy, not a forecast. The ones that get funded are not always the best products — they're the best-prepared founders.

**The cap table chaos.** I've seen founders walk into meetings with investors who ask about their cap table and they literally don't know how much of the company they've given away. They did a SAFE round with a friend, gave some equity to an early advisor, promised shares to a contractor, and never formalized any of it. That's not a minor issue. That's a deal-killer. No sophisticated investor is writing a check into a company that doesn't know who owns what.

**The missing model.** A founder has real traction — good velocity, repeat customers, strong retail presence. But when an investor asks, "What's your monthly cash burn and how long is your runway at current spend?" they don't have a precise answer. They have a rough sense. Roughly isn't good enough. Investors need to see that you understand your cash position down to the month, that you know exactly when you run out of money, and that your raise is sized to give you at least eighteen months of runway.

Every one of these founders had a great product. Every one of them struggled to raise. Not because of the product. Because of the gap.

## Closing the Gap

So what do you actually do about it?

**Start with the financial model.** Build it bottoms-up. Revenue by channel, by account, by SKU. Cost of goods broken down by ingredient, packaging, co-manufacturing, freight, trade spend. Monthly cash flow. Be honest with your assumptions — investors will stress-test them anyway. If you can't build this yourself, find someone who can. This is not optional.

**Get your unit economics cold.** You should be able to answer these questions without looking anything up: What's your gross margin today? What's your path to 45-50%? What's your CAC by channel? What's your LTV? What's your velocity per store per week? What's your repeat purchase rate? Best-in-class CPG brands hit a 4x LTV-to-CAC ratio and a 40% repeat rate. Where do you stand?

**Clean up your cap table.** Document every instrument. Understand your dilution path through three to four rounds. Model out what founder ownership looks like at exit. If you're already over-diluted, that's a conversation to have now, not when an investor points it out.

**Build your deck around investor questions, not your brand story.** What problem are you solving? How big is the market — with bottoms-up validation, not just TAM? What traction do you have — velocity, margin, repeat rate, door count? What's the business model? How does revenue scale? Who's on the team and what have they done before? What are you raising and how will you deploy it? If your deck doesn't answer those questions clearly and concisely, rework it.

**Understand your operational cost structure.** Where is freight on your P&L? Are your co-man fees competitive? Do you have visibility into your 3PL costs? Every dollar you can save by extending payables, shrinking receivables, or reducing inventory is a permanent, one-time cash savings. These details compound. The pennies matter.

## The Shift That Changes Everything

Here's what I've seen happen dozens of times: a founder comes to me struggling to raise. Good product, clear vision, genuine passion. But the infrastructure isn't there. The models aren't investor-grade. The unit economics are fuzzy. The cap table needs work. The deck tells a brand story instead of a business story.

We rebuild the foundation. Get the financial model right. Lock down unit economics so the founder can speak to margins, runway, and growth with precision. Build a deck that shows investors exactly how the revenue is going to scale.

And the fundraising conversation changes completely. Investors aren't on the fence anymore. They can see the path.

That's not magic. That's infrastructure.

I've been involved in forty-four fundraising rounds across my career. The pattern is always the same. The founders who close are the ones who show up prepared — not with the best product, but with the best foundation. The ones who can answer hard questions with specific numbers. The ones who've done the unglamorous work of building the operating infrastructure before they ever walk into the room.

Capital amplifies whatever foundation you've built. If the foundation is solid, capital accelerates growth. If the foundation has cracks, capital accelerates failure.

Don't wait until you need the money to start building these materials. Build them now. Get them right. Then go raise from a position of strength.

I have never successfully sold or financed a company from a position of weakness. And neither will you.

Dream boldly. Plan soberly. Build the infrastructure. Then go get funded.
