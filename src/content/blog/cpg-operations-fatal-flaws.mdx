Most CPG brands don't die because the product was bad. They die because the operations behind the product fell apart. I've seen this over and over again across 8 companies and decades of investing. Brands that had every right to succeed... great product, real consumer demand, solid team... and they still went under because the operational side wasn't there.

Here are the five operational fatal flaws that take them down.

## 1. Overstocking Inventory for Price Breaks

This one gets founders every single time. Your co-manufacturer offers you a 15% discount if you order 50,000 units instead of 10,000. The math looks great on a spreadsheet. And I get the temptation... when you're early and cash is tight, saving $40,000 feels massive.

But here's what the spreadsheet doesn't show you. You're an early-stage brand. Your formula is going to change. Your packaging is going to change. Your SKU mix is going to change. I've watched founders sit on $200K worth of inventory they couldn't sell because they reformulated two months after that big production run. Now they're stuck with product that's either obsolete or approaching its expiration date.

That "savings" just became your most expensive mistake.

Order what you need for 60-90 days. Pay the higher per-unit cost. I know it's painful. But flexibility is worth more than margin at this stage.

## 2. Product Recalls

A recall at any stage is painful. But a recall during fundraising? Catastrophic. Investors are already skeptical of early-stage food brands, and a recall signals that your quality control isn't where it needs to be. That's a risk most investors simply won't take.

And beyond the fundraising impact, a recall destroys retailer trust. Getting pulled from shelves is hard enough. Getting back on those shelves after a recall is nearly impossible at some retailers. I learned early on (the hard way, at Whole Foods, when I didn't even know what a HACCP plan was) that food safety is not something you figure out later.

Invest in quality control from day one. Don't cut corners on food safety. Get your HACCP plan right. Audit your co-manufacturer's facility. Test every production run. The cost of prevention is a fraction of the cost of a recall.

## 3. Running Out of Cash

This is the number one killer of CPG startups. Full stop. And the frustrating part is that it's almost always preventable.

Founders underestimate how long it takes to get paid (net 30 becomes net 60 becomes net 90... and sometimes longer). They underestimate promotional costs. They overestimate velocity. And then the math gets brutal fast. You ship product, wait 60+ days to get paid, and in the meantime you need to fund your next production run, pay your team, and cover slotting fees.

CPG is a "Penny Profit" business, and cash flow is a grind.

Always maintain 18 months of runway. If you have 12 months or less, you should already be fundraising. And build a detailed cash flow model... not a P&L, a cash flow model... and update it weekly. Know exactly when you'll run out of money so you never actually do.

## 4. Overpromising Production Capacity

When you land that big retail account, the temptation is to say yes to everything. Yes, we can fill 500 stores. Yes, we can hit that delivery window. Yes, we can handle that volume. You're so excited about the opportunity that you'll figure out the supply chain later.

But if you can't consistently deliver, you're done.

Retailers have zero tolerance for out-of-stocks. One missed delivery window and you're on probation. Two and you might lose the account entirely. And word gets around... buyers talk to each other (more than you think).

Be straightforward about your capacity. It's better to launch in 100 stores and crush it than to launch in 500 and fail to keep shelves stocked. Scale your supply chain ahead of your distribution, not the other way around.

## 5. Popeye Syndrome

I call this Popeye Syndrome because too many founders think "I yam what I yam" and try to handle everything internally. Finance, marketing, sales, operations, logistics, legal... all on your shoulders. I've been there. I understand the instinct.

But this isn't heroic. It's inefficient, and it leads to burnout and bad decisions. You're not an expert in everything (nobody is), and pretending you are costs you time and money. The founder who insists on doing their own bookkeeping to "save money" is the same founder who misses a critical cash flow problem because they don't know what they're looking at.

Identify the 2-3 things you're genuinely great at and delegate everything else. Use fractional experts... fractional CFO, fractional CMO, outsourced logistics. You don't need full-time people for everything. You need the right expertise at the right time.
