# CPG Demo Strategy and Marketing Spend: Why Your First Dollar Goes to the Shelf

We ran billboards in Manhattan while we had four Whole Foods stores in the entire city.

I'll let that sit for a second.

Four stores. New York City. Billboards.

In the early days of Suja, we were so excited about what we were building that we wanted the world to know. We thought awareness would translate to demand. We thought if people saw the brand, they'd go find it. And maybe some did look... and then couldn't find it anywhere near them. We created awareness with nowhere to convert it. Money in, nothing out.

That mistake taught me a principle I now beat into every founder I work with: **your marketing spend should start at the shelf and work its way out from there.** Not the other way around. The further you get from the shelf, the more speculative your spending becomes... and at early stage, you don't have the luxury of speculative.

## What "Closest to the Shelf" Actually Means

Think about your marketing budget as a series of concentric circles. At the center is the shelf itself, where the transaction actually happens. The further you move from that center, the more assumptions you're stacking.

In-store demos. Trade spend programs. Shelf talkers. Parking lot sampling events at the exact store where your product lives. These are all close to the shelf.

Influencer campaigns. Meta ads. Out-of-home. PR pitches to food editors. These are far from the shelf.

The mistake most emerging CPG founders make is starting in the outer circles. It's understandable. That's what feels like "real marketing." It's visible. It's exciting. You can screenshot an Instagram campaign and show your investors. You can't screenshot a TPR event at Sprouts.

But here's what I know after eight companies and $700 million in exits: if your product isn't moving off the shelf when the consumer is standing right in front of it with their credit card out, no amount of awareness is going to fix that problem. You go too far away from the shelf early on... it just bounces. The consumer sees your ad, gets curious, walks into the store, doesn't see the product or doesn't know what to do with it, and moves on with their life.

You can market your way into trial. You cannot market your way into loyalty. And trial generated far from the point of purchase is the most expensive kind.

## The CEO Who Proved It

A couple of years ago I sat down with the CEO of Celsius. You know the Celsius story. They nearly didn't make it... company was on its last legs around 2012. A financial operator was brought in to turn it around. Here's what he told me about day one:

"I stopped everything away from the shelf on day one and just focused on the shelf. And I didn't get back into it until about three years later."

Three years later, by the way, was around 2021. That's when Celsius went on their hockey stick run. By then he had plenty of money to spend on marketing further from the shelf because the foundation was already built. He earned the right to go wider because he'd already won at the shelf level.

"You go too far away from the shelf," he said, "it just bounces."

Three years of shelf-first discipline before he opened the throttle. That's not timid. That's how you build something that lasts.

## Where Your First Marketing Dollar Should Actually Go

For brick and mortar brands at early stage, 90% of your marketing budget belongs close to the shelf. Here's the priority order:

**Trade spend first.** Your promotional program with retailers is the most direct lever you have on velocity. TPRs, display programs, feature ads in the specific stores where your product already lives. I know it doesn't feel like marketing. But a TPR that drives trial at the exact moment a consumer is standing in front of your product is the most efficient dollar you will ever spend. Don't confuse distribution gains with velocity gains. Getting into more doors means nothing if the product sits. Trade spend drives the thing that keeps you on the shelf: turns.

**Demos second.** In-store sampling is the second most powerful tool you have in the natural channel, bar none. A demo done right converts a curious browser into a first-time buyer on the spot. There is no faster path from awareness to trial... and for an early-stage brand, trial that happens at the shelf converts at a completely different rate than trial that happens because someone saw you on TikTok.

Now. A typical demo runs $250 to $300 before you factor in product cost. That's real money for a brand doing $500K in revenue. Here's a move I've used multiple times: partner with a complementary, non-competing brand to split the cost in half. A beverage brand plus a snack brand. A supplements company plus a wellness food brand. You share the demo staff, split the fee, present to the same consumer at the same moment. You might do slightly less pure solo demos, but you double the total events for the same budget. I've done this a bunch of times. It works.

**Everything else comes later.** Influencer campaigns. Paid social. PR. Out-of-home. These aren't wrong, they're just sequenced wrong when founders lead with them. Amazon and your own website are the one exception because digital retail gives you national distribution from day one... so digital spend there IS close to the shelf. But for physical retail? Stay in the inner circles until you've proven velocity.

## The Battle I'm in Right Now

I'm working with a brand called Prota in the natural channel right now. And my partner in that brand wants to lead with influencers and social. He's never been through a CPG launch before. Good instincts, genuine enthusiasm. And I get it... that's where everyone's attention lives these days.

But I'm insisting on trade spend and demos as the main thrust. Because that's the closest thing to getting the product off the shelf, fast, at this stage. We can go wider later. We need to prove velocity first, build the repeat rate, show the buyer that this thing moves. The broader brand awareness campaigns aren't earned until we've done that work at the shelf level.

This argument happens inside almost every emerging CPG brand I see. And the founders who go wide too early... they'll have gorgeous Instagram grids and shelves going nowhere. Hope is not a strategy. And awareness is not velocity.

CPG is a "penny profit" business. Every dollar has to justify itself against the thing that actually matters: units moved per store per week.

## What to Watch Before You Go Wider

So when does it make sense to start investing further from the shelf?

Watch your velocity first. If you're consistently at or above the category median... if your repeat rate is climbing past 40%... if you've crossed $3 to $5 million in retail revenue and the unit economics are holding together... you've started to earn the right to go further out.

Not before.

The signal is simple: are consumers who find your product coming back for it? If the answer is yes, reliably and measurably, then you can start spending to put more people in front of it. Now awareness spend amplifies a working system. Before that, it's just noise with a bill attached.

At Suja, after we'd proven strong velocity at Whole Foods in Southern California, we started expanding both geographically and in terms of marketing investment. That order mattered. We'd earned the right to spend further from the shelf because we knew what happened when someone tried the product.

## Start at the Shelf. Earn the Right to Go Further.

If you're pre-launch or early stage, the framework is simple. Every marketing dollar you're considering spending, ask yourself: how close is this to the shelf? Is this reaching someone who is physically or digitally one click away from buying?

If yes... prioritize it. If no... table it until you've proven velocity.

The Manhattan billboard we ran at Suja looked great. It cost real money. And it did almost nothing for a brand with four stores in the city. The demos we ran at those four stores moved product. The TPRs we negotiated moved product. That was what got us to fifty stores, then to national.

Work from the inside out. Not the outside in. Everything else is just expensive optimism.
