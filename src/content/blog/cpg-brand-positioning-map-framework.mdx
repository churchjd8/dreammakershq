I still remember the first time James Brennan walked into my office with that dark green bottle.

He'd been doing it for weeks. Setting it on my desk, talking about this cold-pressed juice thing his guys Eric and Bryan were making out of a raw food restaurant in Encinitas. Every time, I waved him off. I'm a guy from the Midwest. Kale juice was not exactly on my radar.

Finally, one afternoon, I tried it.

Three ingredients: apple, kale, and lemon. That's it.

I drove home that evening and told my wife Linda: "Honey, if I -- with my unrefined Midwestern palate -- can love a product with kale in it, then anyone on the planet can."

That wasn't just excitement talking. That was a positioning insight.

What I'd stumbled onto wasn't just a great product. It was a white space. A gap in the market that nobody had cleanly claimed yet: premium organic juice that made healthy living feel accessible, not punishing. "Health without the punishment." A Whole Foods juice bar in a bottle.

That clarity is what we built Suja on. And by September 2012, we launched in 50 Whole Foods stores in Southern California. Three years later, Coca-Cola valued the company at $300 million.

It started with a clear answer to a simple question: *Why you, for who, and why now?*

Most CPG founders I talk to today have never done the real work to find that answer.

---

## The Positioning Problem

Here's what I see constantly.

A founder has a product they love. They have real passion. They get into a few stores. And then they stall.

Not because the product is bad. But because nobody knows what to think about it.

"We make clean, organic snacks for health-conscious families." Okay. So does everyone else on that shelf. What makes you different? Why should a consumer reach for you instead of the brand six inches to your left?

Positioning isn't your tagline. It's not your packaging copy. It's the answer to that question -- and the reason it matters isn't philosophical. It's financial.

"Consistent clarity directly impacts velocity, and velocity earns you shelf space." 

That's not a slogan. That's retail math. If buyers and consumers can't immediately understand why you exist and who you're for, your turns will reflect it. Low turns get you pulled from the planogram. Getting pulled from the planogram is how CPG brands die quietly.

Here's the other thing worth knowing: products are copyable. Brands are not. The second you have a clear, ownable position in the consumer's mind -- that's the thing no competitor can replicate directly. They can copy your formula. They can copy your packaging. They cannot copy what you mean to someone.

## The MAP Framework

Over 30 years and 8 companies, I've used the same framework to evaluate every opportunity and sharpen every positioning. I call it MAP.

**M = Market. A = Audience. P = Positioning.**

Simple letters. Serious rigor inside them.

### M: Market -- Define Your Arena

Most founders define their market way too broadly. "We're in the health food space." That's not a market. That's a vague aspiration. You can't build a strategy around a vague aspiration.

You need to define your market in four nested layers:

1. **Industry** -- Beverages
2. **Category** -- Non-Alcoholic Beverages  
3. **Segment** -- Juice
4. **Niche** -- Organic cold-pressed juice shots

For each layer, you need to know: the actual market size, the five-year CAGR, who the key competitors are, and how that niche fits as a percentage of the broader market.

Why does this matter? Timing.

You want to enter a category that's new but not too new. Where consumers already understand what they're buying, retailers are actively expanding shelf space, and the early leaders have made enough mistakes that there's room to improve on something meaningful.

When we launched Suja in fall 2012, cold-pressed juice was real but nascent. Blueprint Cleanse was essentially the only brand on Whole Foods shelves nationally. Evolution Fresh had just been acquired by Starbucks. We weren't first. We were right on time -- early enough to matter, disciplined enough to execute.

Here's the timing framework I use: a niche growing 20%+ annually signals real opportunity. A niche growing 2% might mean you're too late to find real white space. A niche growing 50% with no current players might mean you're too early -- and you'll burn cash educating a market that isn't ready to buy.

"You don't need to own the ocean. You just need to own a well-defined pool."

Six or seven years after we launched Suja, a Whole Foods buyer told us she had 62 cold-pressed juice brands requesting meetings. She couldn't possibly see them all. That's what a crowded category looks like. We were glad we didn't wait.

### A: Audience -- Define Your Tribe

Here's where I see the second most common failure.

"Our target is health-conscious consumers, 25-45." 

That's not an audience. That's a placeholder. "Health-conscious consumers" is not a tribe. It's a database filter.

Your audience description should be specific enough that you could walk into a Whole Foods and pick your target customer out of a crowd. At Suja, we had it down to a single sentence: "A health-conscious millennial woman, 25-40, living in a metro area, who shops at Whole Foods or Trader Joe's, does yoga or CrossFit, is willing to spend on wellness, and is looking for a convenient way to boost her health because she's too busy to make it herself."

Do you see the difference? We knew her. We knew what she valued, what she feared, what her life looked like at 7:30 on a Tuesday morning. We understood the emotional job she was trying to get done.

The tactic here is simple and free: go stand at the shelf. Talk to people who are actually buying in your category. And read competitor reviews on Amazon -- people will tell you in Amazon reviews things they would never say in a focus group. Unfiltered. Specific. Real.

Also think hard about "badge value." Does someone want to be seen carrying your brand? In certain categories, your product is part of how a consumer expresses their identity. That's not vanity -- that's one of the most powerful positioning angles you can have. Suja bottles on gym bags wasn't an accident.

### P: Positioning -- Stake Your Claim

Now you've earned the right to define your USP.

The template I use: "We're the only [X] that [Y] for [Z]."

Every word matters. Simple. Memorable. Defensible. And it should create a little tension -- if anyone could say it, it isn't a position, it's a generic claim.

Suja's internal positioning during the early years was four words: "A Whole Foods juice bar in a bottle." Not an official tagline. Not something that ever ran in an ad. But everyone on the team knew exactly what we were building. That kind of clarity shapes decisions. Product development. Pricing. Channel sequencing. Who you hire. All of it flows downstream from a positioning that's been properly earned.

Here's how you test whether yours is working:

**The Stranger Test**: Tell a stranger your positioning in one sentence. Can they repeat it back? Can they tell you who it's for and why it's different from everything else out there?

**The Ideal Customer Reaction**: Find three or four people who match your target profile. Show them your positioning. Watch their faces. If you don't get an immediate "oh, that's interesting" -- go back.

**The 24-Hour Test**: Come back the next day. Can they still tell you what you said? Positioning that doesn't stick isn't a position.

And here's a tactical tool that's changed a lot since I started: Pickfu.com. You can present two positioning concepts to 100 matched consumers and get real data back in a few hours for under $100. Consumer testing that used to cost $25,000 in focus groups. Use it.

One more thing on this. "Clarity trumps cleverness, but if you can have both, great." Too many founders chase the witty tagline at the expense of the crystal-clear message. Your customer has three seconds in the grocery aisle. They don't have time for clever. They need to understand immediately.

## Scoring Yourself Honestly

I use a 9-question MAP scorecard -- three questions per letter, rated 1 to 5 each, maximum 45 points. Here's what the scores mean in practice:

- **0-10**: Foggy. You don't have a positioning. You have a wish.
- **11-25**: Getting There. You have a direction but not a destination.
- **26-40**: Sharp. Real traction is possible. You're ready to test.
- **41-45**: Crystal Clear. You're ready to build.

Most first-time founders I work with score somewhere between 15 and 25. Good instincts, real passion, not enough specificity. They've done the emotional work but not the strategic work.

If you don't score at least 26 before you start spending real money on marketing, you're going to waste a significant portion of that money. Hope is not a strategy -- and hope is exactly what a weak positioning is.

---

## Positioning Is the Bridge

A mentor told me something years ago that I've never forgotten: "Positioning is the bridge between brand and economics."

What he meant was that a powerful, clear positioning doesn't just make you sound better in a pitch. It directly drives velocity. Higher velocity keeps you on shelf. Staying on shelf generates syndicated data. Data earns you better placement. Better placement grows your brand. It's a flywheel -- and positioning is what starts it spinning.

We proved it at Suja. "Health without the punishment" wasn't just a warm feeling -- it was behavioral proof. It's why a guy from the Midwest fell in love with a kale juice. It's why Suja crossed $100 million in revenue in five and a half years, with gross margins that grew from 28% at launch to approaching 50%. It's why Coca-Cola and Goldman Sachs put $150 million into the company at a $300 million valuation.

But none of that happens without doing the MAP work first.

Define your market with precision. Know your customer well enough to pick her out of a crowd. Build a positioning that's ownable and specific enough to be a little uncomfortable.

Then test it, sharpen it, and build everything else downstream from it.

Dream boldly. Plan soberly. The positioning is where the plan begins.
