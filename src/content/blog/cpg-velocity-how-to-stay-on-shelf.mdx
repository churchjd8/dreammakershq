At Rowdy Energy, we paid nearly a million dollars in one-time slotting fees to get into Albertsons.

A million dollars. To get on shelf.

Eight months later, they discontinued us.

Velocity was significantly below their hurdle rate. So we were out. And that slotting check? Gone. You don't get that back.

That story still stings a little. Not because losing shelf space is unusual... it happens all the time. It stings because we knew better. We had the distribution data. We could see the velocity numbers creeping along. But we told ourselves a story about the brand needing more time, needing more awareness. We hoped the numbers would turn.

Hope is not a strategy.

## Distribution Is the Headline. Velocity Is the Story.

I've watched founders come back from buyer meetings absolutely lit up. "We got into 800 Kroger stores!" or "We're in Sprouts nationwide!" And I get it, I really do. Distribution feels like winning. It's tangible. You can point to it on a map. You can post about it.

But here's the thing most people don't say out loud: getting distribution is the easiest part.

Don't confuse distribution gains with velocity gains. Distribution tells you how many doors you're in. Velocity tells you whether those doors are actually doing anything. And the retailer? They don't care about your door count. They care about dollars per point of distribution... and they're measuring you against every other brand competing for that same shelf space.

My head of sales Nicky put it perfectly back in the early Suja days. We'd just finished a brutal push to get into new doors, and I told her I couldn't wait to reach full distribution so we could stop worrying about shelf space. She looked at me like I'd lost my mind. "Jeff," she said, "two years from now we'll be fighting tooth and nail to maintain our shelf space." She was right. She was always right about that stuff.

## The Velocity Benchmarks You Actually Need to Know

Most founders don't know where they stand until a buyer tells them they're out. By then it's too late to fix it.

Here's how I think about velocity benchmarking against your category median. These numbers aren't perfect for every category, but they're directionally accurate across most food and beverage retail:

- **80% or above of category median**: You're in a strong position. You can push for expansion, off-cycle resets, secondary placement.
- **60-80%**: Likely able to increase SKU count at the next reset. Stay the course and don't get cute.
- **Around 50%**: You're okay. Not thriving, not dying. But don't take your foot off the gas.
- **30-50%**: You need a real plan. Without one, you probably won't make the next reset.
- **Below 20%**: Something is seriously wrong. Either the product isn't resonating, placement is terrible, the price point is off, or your marketing spend behind that channel is zero.

Most retailers build planograms for roughly 12 months, with a 6-month refresh cycle. Once you're on shelf, you've got about 9 months to prove you belong. In highly competitive categories, sometimes less. That is not a long runway. It goes fast.

## What Low Velocity Is Actually Telling You

When velocity is struggling, founders usually blame one of three things: not enough marketing, not enough demos, not enough brand awareness. Sometimes that's true. But low velocity is often a symptom, not the diagnosis.

Ask these questions first.

Is your product in the right section? Placement matters enormously. Wrong section = wrong shopper. At Suja, one of the early breakthroughs was getting cold-pressed juice moved into the refrigerated produce section at Whole Foods, not buried in the back refrigerated wall where nobody went. That one placement change transformed velocity in those stores.

Is the price point working for that channel? A $9.99 product might fly at Whole Foods but stagnate at a conventional chain where the competitive set is priced at $3.99. We learned this with Suja's own product evolution... we went from a 16-ounce bottle at $9.99 to a 12-ounce at $3.99 for mass adoption. Same brand, very different velocity trajectory.

Is your highest-velocity SKU actually on the shelf? Sounds obvious. But I've walked into countless stores where the wrong flavors were stocked, the top performer was out of stock, and the slowest-moving SKU was front and center. In-store execution is a job. Someone has to own it.

Is the category growing or shrinking? If the whole category is contracting, your velocity suffering might have less to do with your brand than with the macro trend. Know the difference.

## The ACV Trap

Here's a number most founders obsess over that I want to reframe: ACV (All Commodity Volume). ACV measures the percentage of total store sales represented by the stores carrying your product. It sounds like a distribution metric, which it is... but founders often treat it as a proxy for impact.

It's not.

A thousand low-traffic Piggly Wiggly stores will have much lower ACV than 300 Walmart stores. You could be in more doors than your competitor and still reach fewer consumers, generate less revenue, and burn more trade spend doing it. What matters is dollars per point of distribution. That's the number that tells you if your product is actually moving.

This is why channel sequencing matters so much. Build strong presence at Whole Foods, Target, Costco, and Kroger before entering Walmart. Walmart's EDLP model creates pricing tension across channels. And if you're not prepared for the velocity hurdles Walmart sets... you're setting yourself up for a very expensive education.

## How to Actually Move the Number

Okay, so velocity is low. What do you do?

First: figure out what's actually broken before you spend a dollar. Visit stores. Watch shoppers. Is your packaging communicating value quickly? CPG is a "Penny Profit" business, the pennies matter, and so does the three-second look a consumer gives your product before moving on.

Second: talk to your buyer before they talk to you. Raise the issue proactively. Come with data. Come with a plan. Buyers get uncomfortable when brands go quiet and then show up asking for more time. They get energized when you walk in and say "here's what we see, here's what we're doing about it, here's the timeline." Credibility is built in those moments.

Third: get your trade spend working. I've seen brands throw money at national ads when their velocity problem is hyperlocal. Targeted digital ads to zip codes around your stores, in-store demos in the right locations, endcap placement, TPRs (temporary price reductions) in specific doors... these are the levers. Not a Super Bowl ad.

Fourth: consider a portfolio cleanup. Are you spreading your trade spend and attention across 8 SKUs when two of them are doing 80% of your volume? Concentrate. Revenue without margin is ego, and distributing a slow-moving SKU just to have more shelf presence is one of the most expensive vanity moves in CPG.

## The Wellness Shot Lesson

At Suja, we had kombucha on shelf. Technically excellent kombucha. We'd figured out the alcohol level consistency challenge that most brands struggled with. But our velocity told us a story the team didn't want to hear: consumers didn't see Suja as a kombucha brand.

We were at around 12% gross margin on the kombucha (outsourced). Wellness shots, which we produced internally, were generating margins closer to 60%. We made the swap. Company-wide gross margin moved from roughly 32% to roughly 40%. Velocity on the new SKU? About $2,000 per week per club at Costco. The kombucha couldn't touch that.

Gross margin determines destiny. But velocity is what gives you the data to make those margin decisions intelligently.

The right response to low velocity isn't always "spend more." Sometimes it's "remove the SKU and concentrate everything behind what's actually working."

## Distribution Gets You on Shelf. Velocity Earns Your Spot.

The scoreboard for every CPG founder in retail is pretty simple: are you above the retailer's velocity hurdle or below it? Everything else... the awards, the press mentions, the LinkedIn posts about new distribution, the Expo West excitement... none of it matters to the buyer reviewing your movement data in January.

You have 9 months. Make them count.
