I've built 8 companies and invested in dozens more over the past 40 years. And the pattern I keep seeing... the thing that kills me... is founders getting tripped up by legal issues that were completely avoidable. We're not talking about obscure regulatory landmines here. We're talking about basic contract terms that nobody bothered to read.

These are the four legal fatal flaws I've watched destroy early-stage CPG brands. Every one of them was preventable.

## 1. Signing Long-Term Contracts

Here's a rule I live by: never sign a contract longer than one year when you're early-stage. I don't care how good the terms look. Your business is going to change dramatically in the next 12 months. Your co-manufacturer might not be the right fit. Your distributor might not perform. Your broker might not deliver. You might pivot your entire product line (and if you're being honest with yourself, you probably will).

When you lock yourself into a 2-3 year deal, you're handcuffing a company that needs maximum flexibility. I've seen founders stuck paying a co-manufacturer they've completely outgrown because they signed a 3-year agreement to get a slightly better per-unit price. That "savings" cost them hundreds of thousands in opportunity cost.

Keep every contract to 12 months or less. If a vendor won't work with you on those terms, that tells you something about how much they actually value the relationship.

## 2. Ignoring Evergreen Contracts

This one is sneaky. And it catches smart people all the time.

An evergreen contract auto-renews unless you give formal written notice... usually 60-90 days before the renewal date. Miss that window by even one day and you're locked in for another full term. One day.

I've seen founders accidentally renew broker agreements, warehouse leases, and co-manufacturing deals simply because nobody put the cancellation deadline on the calendar. By the time they realized it, they were stuck for another year with a partner that wasn't working. And there's nothing you can do about it. The contract is the contract.

The day you sign anything with an evergreen clause, put the cancellation notice deadline on your calendar with multiple reminders. Treat it like a fundraising deadline, because it is one.

## 3. Giving Personal Guarantees

Don't do it! I've skirted personal guarantees for 40 years, and you can too.

Vendors, landlords, and lenders will push hard for a personal guarantee, especially when your company is small. They'll tell you it's "standard." It's not. It's just what they prefer. And I understand why they want it... it shifts all the risk from them to you.

A personal guarantee means if your company fails, they come after your house, your savings, everything you have. CPG is inherently risky. You're already putting your career and time on the line every single day. Why would you also put your family's financial security on the table?

Push back. Hard. Offer a larger security deposit, prepayment, or shorter initial terms instead. And if they absolutely won't budge? Find another vendor. There's always another vendor.

## 4. Trademark Conflicts

This one can be devastating in a way that's hard to come back from. You spend two years building brand awareness, getting into retail, building a consumer following... and then you get a cease-and-desist letter because another food brand (even in an unrelated category) has a similar name.

Now you're looking at a complete rebrand. New packaging, new marketing materials, new retailer presentations, new consumer education. I've seen this cost brands $500K+ and set them back 18 months. Some never recover. All that brand equity you built? Gone.

Before you spend a single dollar on branding, hire a trademark attorney to do a comprehensive search across all food and beverage categories. Not a quick Google search... a real, thorough trademark search. File your application immediately. The few thousand dollars this costs is the best insurance policy you'll ever buy.
