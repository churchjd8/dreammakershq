Every CPG founder wants to grow fast. I get it. I've been there 8 times. But growing the wrong way is worse than growing slowly. Way worse. Because you burn cash, you burn relationships with retailers, and you burn credibility that took you years to build... all in the name of "growth."

The biggest growth killers aren't a lack of ambition. They're misguided ambition. Here are the four sales and marketing fatal flaws that stall CPG brands.

## 1. Expanding Distribution Too Fast

This is the number one growth trap in CPG, and it's the one I see most often.

You get into a regional retailer and velocity looks decent. So you jump at the national opportunity. Suddenly you're in 3,000 doors with no brand awareness, no marketing support, and no field team to manage it. It feels like progress. It's not.

Here's what happens next. Your product sits on shelves. Velocity drops. The retailer puts you on a performance review. You panic and start running deep promotions to move product, which kills your margins. Within 12 months, you're discontinued in half those stores and your brand has a reputation problem with buyers. And buyers talk to each other.

I've watched this happen with brands that had genuinely great products. Distribution without velocity is just expensive warehousing.

Prove velocity in your home market first. Get to 2-3x category average velocity in one region before expanding to the next. Build brand awareness and consumer demand ahead of distribution, not after. Every door you're in should be a door that's pulling product, not just stocking it.

## 2. Outsourcing Sales Control

Early-stage brands often partner with another company or rely entirely on brokers to handle sales. And the logic makes sense on the surface... you don't have the resources for a sales team, so let someone else handle it.

But here's the problem. Nobody will sell your product with the passion and knowledge that you will. Nobody.

When you hand sales to a third party, you lose control over your retail relationships. You don't know what's being said in buyer meetings. You don't know which stores are being prioritized. And that third party has 50 other brands competing for their attention. Yours is just another line on their P&L.

Keep sales in-house for as long as possible. If you can't afford a full team, at least own the key account relationships yourself. Use brokers for execution and coverage, but never let someone else own the relationship with your most important retail partners. That relationship is your lifeline.

## 3. Premature Platforming

You've got a hit product... say, a cold-pressed juice. So naturally, you decide to launch a line of snack bars, kombucha, and protein shakes. You're "building a platform."

Stop.

You're diluting your focus and your resources. At Suja, we had massive demand to expand into adjacent categories early on. Everyone wanted us to. We resisted. We stayed focused on juice until we absolutely dominated the category. That discipline is what got us to $100M in 5 years.

Every new SKU you launch takes resources away from your core. More complexity in manufacturing, more inventory to manage, more retailer conversations, more marketing spend spread thinner. And if your core product isn't fully maximized yet? You're building on a shaky foundation.

Max out your core category first. When you're the clear leader in your lane... dominant velocity, strong margins, deep retail penetration... then explore adjacent categories. Not before.

## 4. Chronic Over-Promoting

Promotions are a tool, not a strategy. But too many CPG founders use deep discounts as their primary growth lever. Buy one get one free. 50% off. Constant deals on every retailer's app. And the volume looks great... for a while.

Here's what chronic promoting actually does. It trains your consumer to never pay full price. Your "regular" price becomes irrelevant because there's always a deal coming. Your effective price drops 30-40% below list, which destroys your gross margin. And when you try to pull back on promotions? Velocity craters. Because you've built a base of deal-seekers, not brand loyalists.

I've seen brands with fantastic products and terrible margins because they promoted their way to volume instead of earning it through brand building. Your promoted volume isn't real volume. It's rented volume that disappears the second you stop paying for it.

You can market your way into trial, but you cannot market your way into loyalty. Build passionate consumers through product quality, brand storytelling, and community... not discounts. Track your percentage of volume sold on promotion. If it's above 40%, you have a problem.
