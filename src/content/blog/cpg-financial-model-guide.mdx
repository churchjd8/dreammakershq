There was a moment in mid-2018 when my CFO Todd Fisher and I were living inside weekly cash projections... not quarterly, not monthly. Weekly. And what made it surreal was that we were sitting on top of a $100 million revenue business.

Less than $100,000 in the bank. On a hundred million in revenue.

I won't pretend that didn't gut me. But I'll tell you this: we had projections. We had spreadsheets. We had slide decks for investors that showed beautiful hockey sticks going up and to the right. What we didn't have was a financial model that was actually telling us the truth.

That's the thing nobody warns you about early enough. A financial model isn't just a document you build for investors. It's your decision-making infrastructure. If it's wrong, every decision downstream is wrong. And in CPG, where margins are thin and cash cycles are brutal, wrong decisions compound fast.

---

## The Sin Most Founders Commit

I've sat across the table from hundreds of founders over the years. The financial model I see most often is some version of this: "The market is $5 billion. We'll capture 1% of that in year three. That gets us to $50 million." And then there's a revenue line that magically goes from $2 million to $50 million with no real explanation of how.

That's not a financial model. That's a wish list with a spreadsheet attached.

Top-down projections feel confident. They feel big. But investors don't believe them because they can't interrogate them. There's no velocity assumption. There's no door count. There's no trade spend. It's just... aspiration formatted as math.

"Enthusiasm meets math. And math always wins."

The best founders I know build from the shelf up. They start with what they can control and observe, then work outward. Revenue is a result, not a starting point.

## How to Actually Build the Model

Here's the framework I use. It's not complicated, but it requires discipline.

### Step 1: Start With Your Revenue Engine

**Revenue = (Number of SKUs) x (Average door count per SKU) x (Average weekly velocity) x (Average net selling price) x 52**

That's it. That's your revenue line.

Not "we think we'll do $8 million next year." Break it down. How many active SKUs? How many doors is each one in? What is each SKU turning per store per week right now? What do you expect that velocity to be in six months?

If you have two SKUs in 400 stores each, turning an average of 6 units per week, at a $4.50 net selling price... that's $1.12 million. Write it down. Then stress test it. What if velocity drops to 4? What if you only get 300 doors on the new SKU? Build the bear case. The investor will.

Suja launched in 50 Whole Foods stores in Southern California in September 2012. Six months earlier we tasted the first green juice. That's what our model was built around initially: 50 stores, a handful of SKUs, real velocity numbers from our test window. Not a market share capture.

### Step 2: Gross Revenue Down to Net Revenue

Here's where most founders misstate their margins. They calculate gross margin on gross revenue. Wrong.

**Net Revenue = Gross Revenue - Trade Spend - Other Deductions**

Trade spend is contra-revenue. It comes off the top before you get to gross margin. In most conventional grocery channels, you're looking at 15-20% of gross revenue going back out the door in trade: promotions, scans, cooperative advertising, MCBs. At Whole Foods or natural specialty, it's lower. At club, it's structured differently. But it's always somewhere.

If you're not modeling trade spend explicitly from day one, your margins look better than they are. And you'll make pricing decisions, co-man decisions, and growth decisions based on phantom margin.

### Step 3: Build Your Gross Margin Line

**Gross Margin = (Net Revenue - COGS) / Net Revenue**

COGS includes: ingredients and raw materials, packaging (bottles, labels, cases, shrink), co-manufacturing fees, and inbound freight to your warehouse. That's it.

Outbound freight is an operating expense. Slotting fees are an operating expense. Broker commissions are an operating expense. If these are in your COGS, your gross margin is understated and your benchmarking is off.

The benchmarks: 40% gross margin by year two. 50% by year three or four. If you're launching at below 40%, understand how you get there and by when. Investors will ask. And they're right to ask.

At Suja, we started at 28%. We eventually approached 50%. The single biggest move? Cutting our outsourced kombucha business (12% gross margin) and replacing it with wellness shots we produced in-house (60% gross margin). That one decision shifted company-wide gross margin from roughly 32% to 40%. That's what "gross margin determines destiny" actually looks like in practice... a product mix decision that transforms the economics of the whole business.

### Step 4: The Operating Expense Waterfall

Below gross margin, you're building toward EBITDA. The operating expenses in a typical emerging CPG P&L look something like this, as a percentage of net revenue:

- **Sales team and broker commissions**: 8-12%
- **Marketing**: 5-10% (more in early stages, should decline as percentage of revenue as you scale)
- **G&A (admin, finance, HR, legal)**: 5-8%
- **Operations and supply chain**: 4-7%

At scale, a well-run CPG brand should be generating 15-20% EBITDA margins. That's the number strategic acquirers are looking at. That's what makes an exit work.

Most founders look at gross margin and stop there. But gross margin at 50% with operating expenses at 52% of net revenue means you're still losing money. The model has to go all the way down.

### Step 5: Cash and Runway

This is where the model becomes the most important thing you build, and the place where founders look least carefully.

Revenue recognition is not cash collection. You ship the product. Your invoice terms are net 30 or net 60. The distributor pays you... eventually. Meanwhile, your co-man wants payment in 30 days. Your ingredient suppliers want payment in 30 days. And you've got trade deductions coming in months later that you didn't fully anticipate.

CPG is a working capital-intensive business. The faster you grow, the more cash you consume before you generate it. I've seen founders get into a 400-store account, celebrate, and then nearly go under three months later because they didn't model the cash conversion cycle.

Build a 13-week cash flow. Not just a P&L. Model when cash actually comes in and when it actually goes out. Most institutional investors will ask for this, and it will reveal things about your business that the income statement hides.

## What Investors Actually Look At

A lot of founders think investors are evaluating whether the model is right. They're not. Nobody expects a five-year projection to be accurate. What they're evaluating is whether you understand your business well enough to build the assumptions.

Questions investors are really asking:
- Do you know what drives velocity?
- Do you know how long it takes you to get into a door and what it costs?
- Do you understand your trade spend structure across channels?
- Do you know what your gross margin is and what's driving it?
- Have you stress-tested the model and shown us the downside?

When I went through 11 fundraising rounds in Suja's first seven years, the rounds that went smoothly were the ones where I walked in with a model that started with real velocity data, real door counts, and real assumptions about what we expected the next 12-18 months to look like. Not just a $50 million number on a slide.

The rounds that were harder? The ones where we were raising from weakness, where the model was aspirational rather than grounded... those were painful. "Hope is not a strategy" applies to financial models as much as anything.

## A Few Hard-Won Rules

**Build bottom-up, not top-down.** Always. Every time. Market share capture is not a business model.

**Separate gross revenue from net revenue.** The delta is trade spend, and it matters enormously.

**Run your EBITDA scenario from gross margin all the way down.** Gross margin alone doesn't tell the story. The full waterfall does.

**Model cash, not just P&L.** A profitable P&L and an empty bank account can coexist. Ask me how I know.

**Know your velocity assumptions cold.** If you can't defend why you're projecting 8 units per store per week instead of 5, you're not ready to raise.

**Update monthly.** A model that's six months old in a high-growth environment is useless. Keep it live.

---

## The Model Is a Mirror

Here's what building a rigorous financial model actually does for you, beyond fundraising. It forces honesty.

When I was deep in the Suja crisis in 2018, Todd and I would sit down every week with the cash model and ask: what's actually true? Not what did we project last quarter, not what do we want to be true. What's actually true?

Out of that discipline came the decisions that saved the company. We cut marketing by more than $4 million. Counter-intuitive... and revenue grew about 10% the following year. We killed low-margin, outsourced product lines. We shifted resources toward wellness shots. We launched a $4 million raw material cost reduction program. None of those decisions happen without a model that's telling you the truth.

The model isn't a prediction. It's a decision-making tool. The goal isn't to be right. The goal is to be disciplined enough that when things don't go the way you planned, you know exactly what levers to pull.

CPG is a "Penny Profit" business. The pennies matter. And the model is where you find them.
