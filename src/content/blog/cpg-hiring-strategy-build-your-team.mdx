There's a conversation I have with almost every CPG founder I coach, usually around month 12 or 18 when things are just starting to work.

They've got some velocity. They've got a retail footprint. They're starting to believe this thing is actually real.

And they're completely drowning.

So they do what every drowning founder does: they hire someone. Fast. They grab a resume, maybe someone who spent 12 years at Kellogg's or Gatorade or Procter & Gamble, impress themselves with the logo on the letterhead, and put them in charge of something critical. Sales. Operations. Marketing. Whatever fire is burning hottest that week.

Six months later, they're in a deeper hole than when they started.

I've made this mistake. I've watched dozens of founders make it. And I'm telling you now, before you cut that offer letter: the wrong hire at the wrong moment is often worse than no hire at all.

"Hire slow, fire fast." I've said it so many times it's become almost reflexive. But I've never stopped saying it because founders keep ignoring it... right up until the moment it costs them.

## The Real Cost of a Bad Hire

Here's what people don't calculate.

A rushed executive hire at a $100K-$150K salary costs you the salary, obviously. But that's the small number. The real cost is the 6-9 months of organizational drift while they try to figure out a startup environment they weren't built for. The opportunities that slide past while you're managing around their blind spots. The team members who start losing confidence. The retail buyers who feel the inconsistency on the other side of the table.

At Suja, one wrong leader at a critical growth moment cost us more than any single product failure ever did. A product you can fix. A person embedded in your org chart... that's surgery.

"A strong partnership multiplies outcomes. A broken one drains the soul of the business."

And the reason most of these hires go wrong isn't lack of ability. It's lack of fit. That distinction matters.

## The Big Company Trap

This is where CPG founders take the most damage.

You're at $5M in revenue. You hire a VP of Sales who spent 14 years at Nestle. Impressive resume. Conference room credibility. Knows the category backward and forward.

And then... nothing happens.

They want more data before making decisions. They build presentation decks for meetings you don't have time for. They're used to a budget, a support staff, an established playbook. In your company, they ARE the playbook. They have to build it from scratch, often for the first time in their career.

Big CPG experience doesn't automatically translate to startup pace. Maybe 1 in 5 makes the transition cleanly. The other 4 either leave in frustration or, worse, dig in and slow your whole organization down while they wait for infrastructure that isn't coming.

"The team that launches a brand is rarely the team that scales it." That's true. But the corollary is equally true: the team that scales a brand is rarely built from people who only know how to operate inside a machine someone else already built.

What you need in the early days is scrappy first, experienced second. Someone who can make a decision without four approval layers above them. Someone who treats ambiguity as an opportunity, not a problem. Someone who's energized by chaos, not paralyzed by it.

That's a very different profile than what's on most CPG LinkedIn pages.

## The Hiring Scorecard

Before I interview a single candidate for any role, I write the scorecard. Two categories. That's it.

**Technical Competence** is what this person needs to do on day one. Not the aspirational wish list. The table stakes. Can they run a Kroger account without me holding their hand? Can they build a trade spend model? Can they walk into a Whole Foods buyer meeting and hold their own?

**Cultural and Character Traits** is how they behave when things go sideways. Do they take ownership or look for someone to blame? Can they operate with limited resources without falling apart? Do they thrive in the grey areas or freeze up?

The second category is the one most founders skip. They're dazzled by the technical stuff and forget to ask the harder questions.

Here's how I actually interview for character: I don't ask opinions. I ask for stories.

"Tell me about a time you had to improvise with limited resources."

"Describe the best culture you've worked in. What made it work?"

"When did you receive really tough feedback, and what did you do with it?"

Opinions are easy to script. Stories are revealing. You hear who someone actually is in how they describe a hard moment. Whether they take responsibility or deflect it. Whether they talk about what they learned or just what happened.

Write the scorecard before the interview. Not after. Because once a candidate is sitting across from you and they've made you laugh and you like them, it's very hard to stay objective. The scorecard keeps you honest.

## Three Hiring Mistakes I See Over and Over

After 44 fundraising rounds and 8 companies, I've made enough hiring mistakes to fill a chapter. Here are the three that keep showing up:

**Chasing the resume over the fit.** Big logo, wrong stage. I've seen this kill momentum in brands that had everything else going right. The impressive VP turns out to need infrastructure that doesn't exist yet, and you spend three months discovering that.

**Hiring in a hurry.** You're overwhelmed. You need help yesterday. So you rush. A rushed hire creates more work than an empty seat. I mean that literally. You have to manage around their blind spots, repair the damage they do with customers or retailers, and eventually have the uncomfortable conversation you should have had in the first interview.

**Waiting too long to act when it's not working.** This is the one that costs the most. You see the signs. Your team sees the signs. But you keep hoping they'll figure it out. And sometimes they do. But usually they don't. Every extra month you wait is a month of compounding problems.

"Hire slow" gets all the attention. But "fire fast" is where most founders leave money on the table.

When you know, you know. Dragging it out is a form of kindness that costs both of you.

## When to Hire vs. When to Stay Fractional

Not every role needs a full-time employee. Especially in years one through three.

Sales is the one where founders most consistently over-hire too early. You don't need a VP of Sales when you have 50 doors. You need a great broker relationship (I've covered broker strategy separately) and maybe a fractional sales director who can help you navigate it. Bring in month-to-month fractional help when you need a specific capability without the full-time burn rate.

The fatal flaw I see here is a false choice: founders think they have to choose between outsourcing sales entirely or hiring a full internal team. The right answer is usually maintaining internal sales leadership at the strategic level while using brokers for field execution, then hiring internally when velocity actually justifies the headcount.

At Suja, we built the internal team incrementally. Nicky was holding key accounts together before we had real infrastructure. Lauren Soward eventually ran Target, Walmart, and absorbed Trader Joe's at the same time... without adding headcount, because she was exactly the right person for that stage. Jody navigated Kroger and Publix through one of our hardest internal seasons without missing a beat. Gretchen reinvigorated Whole Foods when that relationship needed fresh energy.

None of them succeeded because of a resume. They succeeded because they figured out how to win inside the chaos. That's the trait you're actually hiring for.

## The Lesson That Changed How I Build Teams

I used to think I could hold more than I should. More decisions, more relationships, more accountability. Part of it was ego. Part of it was just speed... you move faster alone in the short term. But you can't build something durable alone.

"I realized how much smarter we are than I am." I say that often because it's genuinely true. The moment you assemble a team that's more capable than you are in their respective domains, and you actually get out of their way, the whole thing accelerates.

The founder's job is to hire people who could teach you something.

That takes humility. It takes patience. It takes a willingness to slow down exactly when the pressure to fill a seat is at its highest.

But it's the difference between a company that survives the growth stages and one that dominates them.

"Mediocre people in positions of influence" is one of the 18 fatal flaws I've documented from decades of building CPG brands. Not because mediocre people are bad people. But because culture is contagious. If you tolerate ego, it spreads. If you reward ownership, that spreads too. What you hire and what you keep tells your whole organization exactly what you actually believe.

Show me your team, and I'll show you what your company is about.
