## Summary
Jeff Church leads a group coaching call with several CPG founders (Mari Mix, Sparkle Buck, a frozen ravioli brand, 5C Beauty, G7 Studio/Odd, Timeless Vodka/Zeal Rum). He gives a detailed walkthrough of his askBabu.ai platform and its "guru" tools, then dives deep into broker strategy for CPG brands -- how to pick them, manage them, negotiate rates, and avoid common mistakes. ## Key Stories & Anecdotes

### The Kilimanjaro Origin of "Babu"
Jeff was climbing Kilimanjaro with his kids about three years ago and was not in shape at all. The guides kept yelling "Come on, Babu! Hurry up, Babu!" -- turns out Babu means "grandpa" in Swahili, and they were telling him to pick up the pace. He adopted it as his grandfather name. His wife is "Safta" (Hebrew for grandma). He says choosing your name as a grandparent is "one of the last free decisions you get to make in your life."

### The Target/Walmart Barcode Price Match Trap
When Jeff launched into Walmart after already being in Target, the Target buyer warned him they would auto-match any barcode prices found in Walmart. Walmart runs everyday low pricing (eDLP), so a product at $2.99 in Target might be $2.49 or $1.99 at Walmart. Target would automatically drop the price to match, keep their same margin, and charge Jeff for the difference -- putting him underwater overnight. Jeff's solution: he launched a different size product for Walmart. His 12 oz product was in Target; he created a 10 oz version for Walmart at a cheaper price. When the Target buyer confronted him -- "Jeff, your product's in Walmart. I told you not to do that" -- he said, "It is a different product. It's a 10 oz six-pack versus a 12 oz." The buyer was amazed: "Oh my god, I can't believe you did that. That's amazing." No issue.

### Selling to Coke Without Invention Assignments
When Jeff went to sell his company to Coke, he didn't have invention assignment agreements signed by all employees. He had to pay about $1,000 per employee to retroactively get valid contracts signed, giving them consideration to assign their rights. Not a massive cost, but an avoidable hassle that came up during due diligence.

### The Celsius CEO Turnaround Lesson
Jeff met with the CEO of Celsius after the turnaround (the company almost failed in 2012). The CEO, a financial guy brought in to save it, told Jeff: "I stopped everything away from the shelf on day one, and I just focused on the shelf. And I didn't get back into it until about three years later" -- around 2021 -- because by then they had plenty of money to spend on marketing further from the shelf. His point: "You go too far away from the shelf, it just bounces."

### The Squeeze Pouch White Space Discovery
Jeff was testing askBabu.ai's competitive analysis tool for a woman doing a squeeze pouch business. The Amazon review scraping feature revealed that overwhelmingly, all squeeze packets leak in some way. She created a leak-proof squeeze package based on that insight and it's been doing really well in about six months since launch.

### Fighting His Partner on Marketing Spend at Prota
Jeff describes actively fighting his partner at Prota over marketing strategy. The partner, who has never been through a CPG launch, wants influencer marketing and things further from the shelf. Jeff is insisting on trade spend and demos in the natural channel as the main thrust because "that's the closest thing to getting the product off the shelf the quickest, early on."

### The First Lawsuit as Your "Best Day"
An attorney told Jeff early in his CPG career: "Your best day is going to be the day you get sued." Jeff thought that was crazy, but the attorney's point was that class action litigators only come after you once you're doing $3-5 million in revenue -- meaning you've actually built something worth going after. Jeff's company had seven lawsuits over 10 years. One was for an energy drink that got sued over Prop 65 (lead in soil) even though the product had extracts with no direct fruit/vegetable content. Cost them $20K to resolve.

### His "Affair with Claudia"
Jeff jokes that he's "having the first affair I've ever had in 37 years of being married. And her name is Claudia, and she's just an Excel model." He's deeply enthusiastic about Claude AI, particularly the Excel plugin, and recommends it to every founder in the group.

## Tactical Advice & Frameworks

### Demo Cost-Sharing Between Non-Competitive Brands
- Typical demo cost: $250-$300+ per demo, plus product
- Partner with a non-competitive brand (beverages + snacks work great) to split the cost in half
- You might "lose a little bit of the quality" but not really, since you'd outsource the demo anyway
- Result: double the number of demos for the same budget
- Jeff has done this "a bunch of times"

### Broker Strategy for Natural Channel
- Some brokers cover the whole natural channel (e.g., Presidents Marketing) -- they tend to charge monthly retainers
- Retainer range: $2,000 to $10,000/month
- At $10K you get better quality and merchandising; at $2K it's "okay too"
- Retainer structure: greater of flat retainer OR 5% of revenue -- once revenue hits that threshold, you stop paying the retainer and just pay commission
- Some brokers will work without a retainer or with a very low one -- "you just got to ferret them out"
- Starting commission rate: 5%, with a goal to negotiate down to 3% over time

### Broker Strategy for Conventional Grocery & Mass
- Do NOT use one-size-fits-all national brokers across conventional grocery and mass
- Use a dedicated broker for each major retailer
- Example: Jeff's Target broker has offices literally across the street from Target headquarters and is in the building every day -- that same broker wouldn't be equally effective for Walmart without a Bentonville presence
- Jeff has broker recommendations for all major retailers and will share them

### What Brokers Should Do For You
1. Account setup (push them on this -- "it's the reason I'm hiring you")
2. Managing promotions (very complicated; askBabu.ai has gurus for this)
3. Resolving distribution issues (e.g., the Target/Walmart barcode problem)
4. Buyer reviews
5. Inventory management
6. Merchandising

### Common Broker Mistakes
1. **Not communicating enough** -- have monthly meetings with your broker; provide a structured meeting agenda
2. **Over-relying on brokers** -- Presidents Marketing reps 100+ brands; you will be out of sight, out of mind if you're passive. "The squeaky wheel gets the grease entirely with this stuff. The pain in the butt brand is going to get a lot more attention."
3. **Failure to monitor performance** -- brokers buy Nielsen data but are contractually restricted from sharing it; you have to be persistent to get information from them
4. **Not training your broker** -- invest in making sure they know how to pitch your product line
5. **Letting brokers control buyer meetings** -- brokers will say "you don't need to come out for the meeting." For top-to-top meetings (pitching new SKUs), you MUST be there. "You want to own that discussion." Brokers won't fight hard on your behalf because they have 12 other brands and don't want to damage their buyer relationship. "They'll push the point, but they'll cave much quicker than you would cave."

### Broker Evaluation Scorecard
Jeff created a formal scorecard with weighted criteria (red = most important):
- How many brands does each account manager carry? (Ideal: less than 8-10; 25 is too many)
- Can they show examples of velocity increases or shelf expansions? (Not just logos -- how did they do it?)
- Will the broker allow you to participate in buyer meetings?
- Score each criterion 0-3 or N/A

### Marketing Spend: Closest to the Shelf First
- Don't spend money on marketing until you have legitimate access to distribution
- Exception: Amazon and your website give you "natural national distribution out of the blocks"
- For brick and mortar: 90% of early marketing spend should be close to the shelf
- Priority order: trade spend program first, then demos (especially in the natural channel)
- Influencer marketing and things "further from the shelf" come later, once you have scale and capital
- Celsius CEO confirmed this approach: stopped all non-shelf marketing on day one of turnaround

### Pricing Strategy: Launch vs. Scale
- Working top-down from market SRP gives you a very low or negative margin at launch (costs are too high)
- Working bottom-up from your target 40% gross margin might price you at $8.99 when the market is $4.99 -- too much stretch
- Sweet spot: price about 20-30% above market at launch, then use promos/discounts to drop into the market range
- "You can always lower your price. It's hard to raise your price."
- At scale, you can typically get ingredient, packaging, and freight costs down to 55-60% of initial launch costs

### Pricing Psychology: The Cliff Effect
- "People's brains are wired for cliff pricing"
- Crossing whole-number thresholds (e.g., $30 to $29, $40 to $39) creates a big velocity kick
- "When they see a three in front of something, they're not as motivated as when they see a two, even though these may be only $3 or $4 apart"

### Contract Best Practices
- Never sign anything longer than one year; Jeff prefers three-month contracts
- "I'm a test and learn person. I'll do three-month contracts all day long."
- Watch for evergreen/auto-renew clauses -- contracts that auto-extend if neither party opts out by a deadline
- Insist on mutual agreement to extend rather than automatic renewal
- Avoid personal guarantees

### FDA/FTC Legal Risk Management
- Get an FDA/FTC attorney to review labels, packaging, and website before launch
- Cost: $1,500-$2,000 for a full review
- Class action litigators don't come after you until you're doing $3-5M in revenue (they need to certify a class and have enough skin in the game)
- You have a 1-2 year halo when launching where you don't have to worry much
- Prop 65 in California: test products for lead once or twice a year if made in California (lead is in the soil)
- Risk tolerance is personal -- being "lily white clean" may hurt your ability to make money, but being completely risk-tolerant means "get ready for lawsuits"

### Founder Agreement Advice
- Create a non-legal, non-binding Memorandum of Understanding (MOU) between co-founders
- Describe expectations for each founder's role and contributions
- "You know it now, but five years from now, you're going to forget it"
- Humans are "somewhat conflict avoidant" and don't force tough issues early

### Don't Launch into Mass Before Natural
- Pursuing Walmart or mass retail before validating in natural, specialty, or club is the "kiss of death"
- Only exception: if you're in a really crowded space where you may have trouble getting shelf space elsewhere

## About askBabu.ai

- URL: askBabu.ai
- Free during beta; always free for Jeff's coaching program members with premiere access
- Built over 15 months at a cost of ~$200K with a development team in India
- Uses Claude as its primary LLM backend (has also tested Perplexity, ChatGPT, Gemini)
- Contains ~6,000 curated CPG documents: articles, podcasts, books, accelerator/bootcamp content, LinkedIn scrapes
- Data privacy: nothing is shared to the internet, no robot scraping, no data used for LLM training -- equivalent to paid-tier privacy on ChatGPT
- Deep research runs ~23 minutes (7 minutes longer than competitors) with extra time focused on reducing hallucinations by triangulating and verifying sources
- Short research: ~8 minutes, produces 5-10 page response
- Deep research: produces 20-30 page response
- Has an API that scrapes Amazon reviews to find competitive white space
- ~40 "guru" tools covering finance, marketing, operations, legal
